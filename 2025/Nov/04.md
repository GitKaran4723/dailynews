## Karnataka‚Äôs ‚ÄòDeep Tech Decade‚Äô Initiative

### 1. **Basic Understanding**

**Deep Tech** refers to innovations based on advanced scientific and engineering breakthroughs ‚Äî unlike ‚Äúshallow tech‚Äù such as e-commerce apps or social media, deep tech deals with core areas like **Artificial Intelligence (AI)**, **Machine Learning (ML)**, **Quantum Computing**, **Robotics**, **Semiconductors**, **Advanced Manufacturing**, and **Clean/Sustainable Technologies**.
Such innovations often require **high R&D investment**, long gestation periods, and strong linkages between **academia, government, and industry**.

Karnataka ‚Äî particularly Bengaluru ‚Äî has long been India‚Äôs technology hub, home to **over 40% of India‚Äôs IT exports**, and hosts a large number of **startups, research institutions, and multinational tech centres**. The State aims to now evolve beyond traditional IT services and dominate the **deep-tech ecosystem**, which will shape the next wave of industrial and digital revolutions.

# India‚Äôs IT dream is at a crossroads
## India‚Äôs IT Sector: From Mass Employment to Deep-Tech Reinvention

---

### 1. **Basic Understanding**

India‚Äôs **Information Technology (IT) sector** has been a cornerstone of the country‚Äôs economic rise since the 1990s. It transformed India from a service-dependent economy to a global **technology powerhouse**, contributing nearly **7% of India‚Äôs GDP** and employing **6 million people**.

Key IT companies such as **Infosys, TCS, Wipro, and HCL Technologies** helped establish India‚Äôs reputation as the **world‚Äôs outsourcing hub**, providing cost-efficient software development, maintenance, and business process outsourcing (BPO) services.

However, the **technological landscape is changing**. With the advent of **Artificial Intelligence (AI)**, **Machine Learning (ML)**, **cloud computing**, and **automation**, traditional coding and support jobs are being automated or replaced. The sector that once promised stable, long-term careers now faces **massive restructuring** ‚Äî a shift from ‚Äú**manpower-based**‚Äù to ‚Äú**mindpower-based**‚Äù growth.

---

### 2. **The News**

In late 2025, **Tata Consultancy Services (TCS)** announced its **largest-ever layoff** ‚Äî nearly **20,000 employees** in one quarter ‚Äî signaling a major disruption in India‚Äôs IT industry. Other IT giants have followed suit, with **industry-wide job losses expected to cross 50,000** by the end of the fiscal year.

This wave of layoffs, termed **‚Äúsilent layoffs‚Äù**, includes **performance-linked exits, delayed promotions, and voluntary resignations** rather than outright dismissals.

Globally, similar trends are visible:

* **Amazon** laid off **14,000** employees.
* **Meta** dismissed **8,000** workers.
  These changes reflect a global **AI-driven transformation** in how companies manage human resources and productivity.

In India, the crisis is compounded by:

* **AI automation** reducing the need for low- to mid-level coding roles.
* **Restrictive U.S. visa and tariff policies** affecting Indian firms‚Äô overseas operations.
* **Shrinking client budgets** in Western economies due to uncertainty.

The traditional **‚Äúdigital assembly line‚Äù model** ‚Äî hiring thousands of engineers for repetitive coding ‚Äî is now obsolete. Clients now demand **AI-powered solutions, cybersecurity systems, and cloud-native architectures**, requiring **fewer but more skilled professionals**.

---

### 3. **Government Interventions & Initiatives**

**Existing Frameworks & Schemes:**

* **Digital India (2015):** Focused on improving digital literacy and e-governance.
* **Skill India Mission (2015):** Promotes large-scale upskilling and vocational training.
* **FutureSkills PRIME (NASSCOM & MeitY):** National digital skilling platform to train professionals in AI, data science, and cybersecurity.
* **IndiaAI Mission (2023):** Aims to democratize access to AI tools, establish compute infrastructure, and build a national AI innovation ecosystem.
* **Production-Linked Incentive (PLI) Schemes:** Support domestic electronics and semiconductor manufacturing, indirectly boosting the tech ecosystem.
* **Startup India & Deep Tech Decade (Karnataka, 2025):** State-led innovation and entrepreneurship missions supporting deep-tech ventures and AI-based startups.

**Future Government Interventions (Suggested or Needed):**

* **Mandatory severance policies**: Require firms engaging in large-scale layoffs to provide 6‚Äì9 months‚Äô salary as safety net.
* **Curriculum reform in engineering colleges** to align education with AI, cloud, and data science needs.
* **Public‚ÄìPrivate Skilling Partnerships** to accelerate upskilling and reskilling.
* **Support for AI & Deep-Tech Startups** through funding and incubation.

---

### 4. **Mains Relevance**

**Paper Links:**

* *GS Paper 3: Science & Technology, Economy, Employment & Skill Development.*

**Issues and Challenges:**

1. **Technological Disruption:**

   * AI and automation reducing routine IT jobs.
   * Threat to traditional service-based employment.

2. **Skill Obsolescence:**

   * Mid-career professionals with outdated technical knowledge (e.g., legacy systems like SAP ECC).
   * Limited AI and data science expertise among the current workforce.

3. **Economic and Employment Concerns:**

   * Short-term unemployment and reduced job security in the IT sector.
   * Shrinking entry-level hiring in campus placements.

4. **Global Dependencies:**

   * U.S. visa restrictions and trade uncertainty affecting the outsourcing model.

5. **Policy Gaps:**

   * Lack of social safety nets (unemployment benefits, retraining support).

**Impacts:**

* **Economic:** Structural shift from low-cost coding to high-value AI-driven innovation.
* **Social:** Rising unemployment and anxiety among mid-career IT professionals.
* **Technological:** Opportunity for India to pivot towards deep-tech and AI leadership.
* **Ethical:** Challenges in responsible AI deployment and workforce displacement.

### 5. **Way Forward**

**1. Reimagine Skilling and Education:**

* Update curricula in engineering and technical institutions to focus on AI, data science, quantum computing, and cybersecurity.
* Introduce interdisciplinary courses combining technology with ethics, design, and policy.

**2. National AI Upskilling Mission:**

* Create a government-led mission to reskill millions of workers for the AI economy.
* Incentivize companies that provide structured reskilling programmes.

**3. Strengthen Public‚ÄìPrivate Partnerships:**

* Encourage industry-academia collaborations for R&D and innovation.
* Support joint AI research centers and technology parks.

**4. Protect Workers and Ensure Transition Support:**

* Introduce legal provisions for **mandatory severance pay** and **career transition assistance**.
* Provide **tax incentives for reskilling investments** by companies.

**5. Diversify India‚Äôs Tech Ecosystem:**

* Promote **deep-tech entrepreneurship** and **product-based innovation** beyond Bengaluru.
* Encourage local innovation clusters and tech incubators in Tier-II and Tier-III cities.

**6. Global Engagement and Policy Alignment:**

* Negotiate better **visa and data policies** with key markets.

If guided by sound policy, sustained reskilling, and strategic vision, India‚Äôs IT story can evolve from a ‚Äúback office of the world‚Äù to a **frontline innovator in the age of AI**.

# A Kerala story
## Kerala Eradicates Extreme Poverty: A Landmark in Human-Centred Development

---

### 1. **Basic Understanding**

**Extreme Poverty** refers to a condition where individuals or families lack the basic necessities for survival ‚Äî food, shelter, healthcare, and means of livelihood. In global terms, the World Bank defines it as living on less than **$2.15 per day (PPP)**. In India, the **Multidimensional Poverty Index (MPI)** ‚Äî developed by NITI Aayog using parameters like **health, education, and standard of living** ‚Äî is used to assess deprivation beyond income.

**Kerala‚Äôs Development Model**, often called the **‚ÄúKerala Model‚Äù**, emphasizes **human development indicators (HDIs)** such as literacy, healthcare, gender equality, and social welfare, rather than just economic growth. This model has made Kerala comparable to developed nations in terms of **social progress**, despite its modest per capita income.

On **November 1, 2025 (Kerala‚Äôs 69th Formation Day)**, the State government announced a historic achievement ‚Äî the **eradication of extreme poverty**, marking a culmination of its **four-year-long Extreme Poverty Eradication Programme (EPEP)**.

---

### 2. **The News**

* **Event:** Kerala declared **eradication of extreme poverty**.
* **Date:** November 1, 2025 (Kerala Formation Day).
* **Programme:** **Extreme Poverty Eradication Programme (EPEP)**, launched in **May 2021** by the **Pinarayi Vijayan-led LDF government**.
* **Lead Agency:** Department of Local Self-Government, with **Kudumbashree** and **community participation**.
* **Duration:** 4 years.
* **Scale:**

  * **Enumerators:** 4 lakh trained personnel.
  * **Beneficiaries Identified:** 64,006 families (1,03,099 individuals).
  * **Criteria:** Access to **food**, **health**, **livelihood**, and **housing**.
* **Support Provided:** Customised micro-plans including ID documents, palliative care, cooked meals, medicines, housing, and even **organ transplants**.

**Follow-up Initiative:**
To prevent relapse, the government has launched **EPEP 2.0**, ensuring continuous monitoring and social safety.

**Background Data:**

* Poverty in Kerala declined from **59.8% (1973‚Äì74)** ‚Üí **11.3% (2011‚Äì12)**.
* **NITI Aayog‚Äôs MPI 2023:**

  * Kerala = **least impoverished State** (only **0.55%** multidimensionally poor).
  * India average = **14.96%**.

This achievement stands as a **global example of inclusive governance**, blending welfare and developmental economics.

---

### 3. **Government Interventions & Initiatives**

**1. Extreme Poverty Eradication Programme (EPEP):**

* Focused on **identifying and supporting the abjectly poor** using local governance mechanisms.
* Implemented through **Panchayats**, **municipalities**, **Kudumbashree (State poverty eradication mission)**, and **civil society**.
* Each family received **customised micro-plans** to address individual needs.

**2. Kudumbashree Mission (1998‚Äìpresent):**

* World-renowned women-led self-help network with over **45 lakh members**.
* Plays a vital role in poverty alleviation, microfinance, and entrepreneurship.

**3. Decentralised Planning (People‚Äôs Plan Campaign, 1996):**

* Strengthens **local self-governance** by devolving financial and administrative power to Panchayati Raj Institutions (PRIs).

**4. Social Safety and Welfare Schemes:**

* **Karunya Benevolent Fund**, **Life Mission (housing)**, **Aardram Mission (healthcare)**, **Subhiksha Keralam (food security)**.
* Integration of **welfare databases** for better targeting.

**5. EPEP 2.0 (2025):**

* Ensures that no family falls back into extreme poverty.
* Focuses on **sustainability, self-reliance, and income generation**.

---

### 4. **Mains Relevance**

**Relevant Papers:**

* *GS Paper 2:* Welfare Schemes for Vulnerable Sections, Role of Local Governments, Human Development.
* *GS Paper 3:* Inclusive Growth and Social Sector Initiatives.

**Issues and Challenges:**

* **Verification Gaps:** Difficulty in ensuring accuracy of beneficiary identification.
* **Tribal and Marginalised Populations:** Persistent deprivation among certain groups, especially in remote regions.
* **Employment & Growth Paradox:** Kerala‚Äôs social progress contrasts with **slow industrial growth** and **high educated unemployment**.
* **Fiscal Stress:** High welfare expenditure can strain state finances.
* **Dependency Risk:** Long-term welfare may discourage labour market participation if not linked to productivity.

**Significance and Impact:**

* **Social:** Strengthened inclusivity, social cohesion, and gender empowerment.
* **Economic:** Encourages productive employment through livelihood interventions.
* **Governance:** Demonstrates success of **bottom-up, participatory planning**.
* **National Relevance:** Provides a **replicable model** for other states with high poverty ratios (e.g., Bihar, Jharkhand, UP).
* **Global Perspective:** Aligns with **UN SDG 1 (No Poverty)** and **SDG 10 (Reduced Inequalities)**.

**Ethical & Governance Dimensions:**

* Reinforces the idea that **welfare and growth are not mutually exclusive**.
* Promotes transparency, accountability, and empathy in governance.

---

### 5. **Way Forward**

1. **Sustain Gains through EPEP 2.0:**

   * Continuous monitoring of vulnerable families through digital dashboards.
   * Focus on **preventive poverty governance**, not just curative welfare.

2. **Address Tribal and Regional Disparities:**

   * Special outreach missions for **Adivasi and coastal communities**.
   * Improve access to land rights, healthcare, and education.

3. **Link Welfare with Livelihood Creation:**

   * Integrate welfare beneficiaries into **micro-enterprise networks** and **cooperative models**.
   * Leverage **Kudumbashree enterprises** for rural economic revival.

4. **Promote Skill Development and Employment:**

   * Align with **Kerala Knowledge Economy Mission** to provide high-tech jobs.
   * Encourage startups and green industries to tackle unemployment.

5. **Fiscal Prudence and Sustainability:**

   * Encourage **public-private partnerships (PPPs)** for welfare delivery.
   * Rationalise subsidies and ensure targeted assistance.

6. **Replication at the National Level:**

   * The **EPEP model** can be adapted by other states using **district-level poverty audits** and **community micro-planning**.

---

### üß≠ **Conclusion**

Kerala‚Äôs declaration of the **eradication of extreme poverty** marks a **historic milestone in India‚Äôs welfare and governance journey**. It exemplifies how **decentralised governance, participatory planning, and social commitment** can together achieve what top-down programmes often fail to do.

While challenges such as unemployment and tribal deprivation remain, **Kerala‚Äôs experiment in human-centric governance offers a replicable blueprint** for inclusive development. The ‚ÄúKerala Model‚Äù ‚Äî blending **welfarism with growth and sustainability** ‚Äî stands as a reminder that the true measure of progress lies not in GDP, but in **dignity, equality, and human well-being**.

# The case for energy efficiency
## India‚Äôs Clean Energy Paradox: Why the Grid Is Getting Dirtier Despite More Renewables

---

### 1. **Basic Understanding**

India‚Äôs **energy transition** is one of the most ambitious in the world ‚Äî shifting from fossil fuels to **clean, renewable sources** like **solar, wind, hydro, and nuclear**. The country has achieved remarkable growth in renewable energy (RE) capacity: as of **June 2025**, non-fossil fuel sources account for **~50% of total installed capacity**.

However, a paradox has emerged: **India‚Äôs electricity is becoming more carbon-intensive**, not less. The **Grid Emission Factor (GEF)** ‚Äî the amount of CO‚ÇÇ emitted per megawatt-hour (MWh) of electricity ‚Äî has **risen from 0.703 tCO‚ÇÇ/MWh in 2020‚Äì21 to 0.727 tCO‚ÇÇ/MWh in 2023‚Äì24**, according to the **Central Electricity Authority (CEA)**.

This means that even though the country has installed more renewable capacity, the **actual electricity consumed** (generation) still relies heavily on **coal**, making the grid ‚Äúdirtier.‚Äù Understanding this paradox requires grasping the **capacity‚Äìgeneration mismatch** and the role of **energy efficiency** in balancing the transition.

---

### 2. **The News**

* **What Happened:** Despite record renewable energy installations, India‚Äôs grid has become more carbon-intensive over the past five years.
* **Data:**

  * **GEF increased** from **0.703 ‚Üí 0.727 tCO‚ÇÇ/MWh (2020‚Äì21 to 2023‚Äì24)**.
  * **Installed non-fossil capacity:** ~50% of total.
  * **Renewable share in electricity generation:** only **22% (2023‚Äì24)**.
* **Reason:**

  * Renewables like solar and wind have **low capacity utilisation (15‚Äì25%)**, while coal runs at **65‚Äì90%**.
  * **Electricity demand peaks at night**, when solar is unavailable, forcing **coal plants** to compensate.
  * Thus, although installed renewable capacity is large, **coal still delivers most of the electricity**.

This mismatch between **installed capacity** and **actual generation** ‚Äî coupled with India‚Äôs **rapidly rising electricity demand** ‚Äî is driving up emissions from the grid.

---

### 3. **Government Interventions & Initiatives**

#### **A. Clean Energy and Decarbonisation Policies**

1. **National Electricity Plan (NEP) 2023:**

   * Projects India‚Äôs GEF to decline to **0.548 by 2026‚Äì27** and **0.430 by 2031‚Äì32**.
   * Focus on renewable energy expansion, storage, and flexible grid operations.

2. **National Solar Mission (2010)** and **National Wind-Solar Hybrid Policy:**

   * Targets 500 GW of non-fossil fuel capacity by 2030.

3. **Green Hydrogen Mission (2023):**

   * Aims to decarbonise industrial and transport sectors using hydrogen produced from renewable electricity.

4. **Energy Conservation (Amendment) Act, 2022:**

   * Establishes **Carbon Credit Trading Scheme** and mandates **energy efficiency standards** for industries.

5. **Perform, Achieve, and Trade (PAT) Scheme ‚Äì Bureau of Energy Efficiency (BEE):**

   * Promotes industrial energy efficiency through tradable efficiency certificates.

#### **B. Energy Efficiency Achievements**

* From **FY2017‚Äì18 to FY2022‚Äì23**, India saved:

  * **200 Million Tonnes of Oil Equivalent (MTOE)** of final energy,
  * Equivalent to **1.29 GtCO‚ÇÇe** avoided,
  * **‚Çπ7.6 lakh crore** in monetary savings.
* Efficiency has proven to be India‚Äôs **‚Äúfirst fuel‚Äù** ‚Äî the cleanest and cheapest energy source is the energy not used.

#### **C. Other Supportive Measures**

* **Ujjwala Yojana & Saubhagya:** Reduce household dependence on biomass, increasing grid demand but improving health outcomes.
* **Smart Metering & Grid Modernisation (Revamped Distribution Sector Scheme ‚Äì RDSS):** Enable demand-side management and time-of-use pricing.
* **Round-the-Clock (RTC) Renewable Power Bids:** Combine renewable sources with storage to ensure reliable green supply (< ‚Çπ5/kWh).

---

### 4. **Mains Relevance**

**Papers:**

* *GS Paper 3 ‚Äì Environment, Energy, Infrastructure, and Economy.*

#### **Issues and Challenges**

1. **Capacity‚ÄìGeneration Mismatch:**

   * Renewables provide ~50% capacity but only 22% actual generation.

2. **Coal Dependence:**

   * India remains one of the world‚Äôs largest coal users; 70% of power generation is coal-based.

3. **Intermittency and Storage:**

   * Lack of large-scale storage limits renewable integration.

4. **Peak Demand Timing:**

   * Renewable availability (daytime) mismatched with peak demand (evening).

5. **Transmission Bottlenecks:**

   * Delays in inter-state green energy corridors hinder renewable evacuation.

6. **Policy Fragmentation:**

   * Efficiency, renewables, and demand management often handled by separate agencies without holistic coordination.

#### **Impacts**

* **Economic:** High coal imports and subsidies increase fiscal burden.
* **Environmental:** Rising GEF undermines India‚Äôs climate goals and Paris commitments.
* **Social:** Increased local pollution from coal plants; affects health and livelihoods.
* **Technological:** Pushes innovation in storage, grid flexibility, and demand-side management.

#### **Ethical & Governance Aspects**

* Balancing **energy access, affordability, and sustainability**.
* Ensuring a **just transition** for coal-dependent regions and workers.

---

### 5. **Way Forward**

1. **Make Energy Efficiency the Core Strategy:**

   * Enforce stricter **appliance efficiency standards (4- and 5-star products)**.
   * Mandate **energy audits** for high-consumption industries.
   * Provide **scrappage incentives** for old, inefficient equipment.

2. **Enable Flexible and Smart Grids:**

   * Promote **virtual power plants (VPPs)** integrating home batteries and EVs.
   * Expand **battery and pumped hydro storage** for round-the-clock renewable supply.
   * Implement **real-time pricing** to encourage consumption when renewables are abundant.

3. **Support Energy-Efficient SMEs:**

   * Subsidies and low-interest loans for replacing old motors, furnaces, and pumps.

4. **Integrated Planning:**

   * Align renewable deployment, storage, transmission, and demand forecasting under one **National Energy Transition Framework**.

5. **Behavioural and Institutional Change:**

   * Launch mass awareness campaigns for energy conservation (similar to ‚ÄúSwachh Bharat‚Äù or ‚ÄúUjjwala‚Äù).
   * Encourage state DISCOMs to procure **‚Äúenergy services‚Äù** (like efficient cooling) instead of raw electricity.

6. **Long-Term Vision:**

   * Focus on **grid flexibility, decentralised renewables**, and **demand response** instead of just capacity addition.
   * Make **efficiency the ‚Äúfirst fuel‚Äù** and **flexibility the backbone** of India‚Äôs decarbonisation journey.

---

### üß≠ **Conclusion**

India‚Äôs rising **Grid Emission Factor** reveals a crucial truth: installing more renewables alone doesn‚Äôt guarantee cleaner power. Without flexibility, storage, and large-scale efficiency, renewables remain underutilised, and coal continues to dominate peak generation.

The path to a **low-carbon, resilient energy future** lies not in abandoning coal overnight, but in **redesigning the grid, empowering consumers, and institutionalising efficiency**. If renewables are the heart of India‚Äôs clean energy future, **efficiency must be its spine** ‚Äî invisible, essential, and powerful.

# Need for shift in focus from food security to nutrition security: PM
## Emerging Science, Technology and Innovation Conclave (ESTIC): PM Modi‚Äôs Vision for Future-Ready Science

---

### 1. **Basic Understanding**

The **Emerging Science, Technology and Innovation Conclave (ESTIC)** is a new national platform aimed at fostering collaboration between **scientists, innovators, policymakers, and industry leaders**. It seeks to align India‚Äôs **scientific research** with **strategic national priorities** ‚Äî such as food and nutrition security, clean energy, biotechnology, and sustainable development.

India has made rapid advances in science and technology ‚Äî from space exploration (Chandrayaan-3, Aditya-L1) to digital innovation (Digital India, UPI, Aadhaar). However, **translating scientific progress into inclusive societal outcomes** remains a challenge. ESTIC aims to bridge this gap by bringing together diverse stakeholders to co-create solutions for **21st-century challenges** like climate change, malnutrition, biodiversity loss, and resource constraints.

---

### 2. **The News**

* **Event:** First **Emerging Science, Technology and Innovation Conclave (ESTIC)**.
* **Date:** Monday (November 3, 2025).
* **Occasion:** Inaugural address by **Prime Minister Narendra Modi**.
* **Location:** New Delhi.
* **Key Focus Areas Highlighted by the PM:**

  1. **From Food Security to Nutrition Security:** Scientists should work on **biofortified and nutrient-rich crops** to combat malnutrition.
  2. **Low-Cost Fertilisers:** Develop affordable and sustainable alternatives to reduce farmer dependence on chemical imports.
  3. **Genomic Mapping for Personalised Medicine:** Build a comprehensive map of **India‚Äôs genetic biodiversity** to advance precision healthcare and preventive medicine.
  4. **Innovation in Clean Battery Storage:** Create **low-cost, indigenous battery technologies** to power India‚Äôs clean energy transition.
  5. **Science for the People:** Strengthen linkages between **research institutions, startups, and industries** for scalable innovation.

The Prime Minister emphasized that Indian science must not only pursue excellence but also **‚Äúsolve India‚Äôs local problems and inspire global solutions.‚Äù**

---

### 3. **Government Interventions & Initiatives**

#### **1. Science and Technology Policy Framework**

* **Science, Technology, and Innovation Policy (STIP 2020‚Äì21):**

  * Promotes ‚ÄúOpen Science‚Äù and ‚ÄúAtmanirbhar Research.‚Äù
  * Aims for **India‚Äôs R&D investment to reach 2% of GDP**.
* **National Research Foundation (NRF) Act, 2023:**

# Has cloud seeding been effective?
## Cloud Seeding in Delhi: Science, History, and India‚Äôs Experiments

---

### 1. **Basic Understanding**

**Cloud seeding** is a **weather modification technique** designed to induce or enhance rainfall by dispersing substances (usually salts) into clouds to encourage condensation and precipitation.

**Scientific Principle:**

* Clouds consist of **tiny water droplets or ice crystals** suspended in the atmosphere.
* These droplets are often too small to fall as rain unless they **aggregate or grow larger**.
* In cloud seeding, aircraft (or rockets) release **chemical agents** like **silver iodide, potassium iodide, sodium chloride, or calcium chloride** into suitable clouds.
* These particles act as **nuclei** around which moisture condenses or freezes, eventually forming **raindrops or snowflakes** that fall to the ground.

**Common Methods:**

1. **Aerial Seeding:** Aircraft release flares containing the seeding material into the cloud base or top.
2. **Ground-Based Seeding:** Rockets or generators release seeding agents that are carried upward by air currents.

**Typical Uses:**

* Enhancing rainfall in **drought-prone areas**.
* **Reducing smog** or dust by washing out pollutants.
* **Suppressing hail** or dispersing fog (especially around airports).

---

### 2. **The News**

* **Event:** For the first time in nearly 50 years, **Delhi conducted two cloud seeding trials** with the **Indian Institute of Technology, Kanpur (IITK)**.
* **Date:** October 28, 2025.
* **Objective:** To induce rainfall over Delhi to **settle smog and particulate matter** that had severely degraded the city‚Äôs air quality.
* **Process:**

  * IIT Kanpur used its aircraft fitted with flares containing **salt mixtures**.
  * The flares were fired into clouds over Delhi to stimulate rain formation.
* **Outcome:**

  * Only a **light drizzle** was reported in parts of Delhi.
  * **No significant rainfall** occurred due to **poor cloud quality** (insufficient moisture in post-monsoon clouds).
* **Significance:**

  * This was Delhi‚Äôs first serious cloud seeding attempt since 1962.
  * It marks a new experimental phase in India‚Äôs weather modification research.

---

### 3. **Government Interventions & Initiatives**

#### **A. Early Indian Experiments**

* **1952:** First cloud seeding experiment in **Kolkata**, led by **S.K. Banerji**, India Meteorological Department (IMD).
* **1962:** Attempted seeding in Delhi ‚Äî unsuccessful.
* **1970s onwards:**

  * Several drought-hit States (Tamil Nadu, Karnataka, Maharashtra, Andhra Pradesh) conducted periodic experiments using aircraft and rockets.
  * Most results were **inconclusive** due to lack of standardised methods or reliable measurement tools.

#### **B. CAIPEEX (Cloud Aerosol Interaction and Precipitation Enhancement Experiment)**

* **Launched by:** Indian Institute of Tropical Meteorology (IITM), Pune in **2009**.
* **Objective:** To scientifically study the impact of cloud seeding on rainfall.
* **Location:** **Solapur, Maharashtra** (drought-prone region).
* **Method:**

  * Compared **seeded and unseeded clouds** using radar and aircraft.
  * Used **calcium chloride**, avoiding silver iodide for environmental safety.
* **Findings:**

  * Seeded clouds yielded **46% more rainfall** on average.
  * Over a **100 sq. km downwind area**, there was **18% more rainfall**.
  * Estimated **867 million litres** of additional water generated.
* **Outcome:** Demonstrated measurable rainfall enhancement potential under **favourable monsoon cloud conditions**.

#### **C. Institutional Involvement**

* **India Meteorological Department (IMD):** Provides weather data and identifies seedable clouds.
* **IIT Kanpur, IITM, and State Governments:** Lead scientific and operational seeding programs.
* **Ministry of Earth Sciences (MoES):** Oversees research on cloud microphysics and weather modification.

---

### 4. **Mains Relevance**

**Relevant Papers:**

* *GS Paper 3 ‚Äì Environment, Science & Technology, Disaster Management, and Pollution Control.*

#### **Issues and Challenges**

1. **Scientific Uncertainty:**

   * Difficult to prove if rainfall occurred due to seeding or natural variation.
   * Highly **dependent on cloud type, moisture content, and temperature**.

2. **Seasonal Constraints:**

   * Works best on **moist monsoon clouds**, less effective in **dry post-monsoon** or **winter conditions**.

3. **Environmental Concerns:**

   * Use of **silver iodide** can pose ecological risks if not properly regulated.
   * Long-term environmental effects remain under-researched.

4. **Economic Viability:**

   * Cloud seeding operations are **expensive** (aircraft, pilots, chemicals, radar systems).
   * **Cost-benefit ratio** remains uncertain for large-scale implementation.

5. **Policy and Regulation:**

   * India lacks a **national policy on weather modification**, leading to ad hoc experiments by States.

#### **Potential Benefits**

* **Pollution Control:** Artificial rain can temporarily **reduce smog and PM2.5 levels** in urban centres like Delhi.
* **Water Augmentation:** Could supplement water supplies in drought-prone or rain-deficit regions.
* **Agricultural Security:** May help during **monsoon failures** to protect crops.
* **Scientific Development:** Strengthens India‚Äôs **cloud physics and meteorological research** capabilities.

#### **Ethical & Governance Dimensions**

* Need for **scientific transparency**, data sharing, and **environmental impact assessments (EIAs)** before large-scale seeding.
* Avoid politicisation or public over-expectation of cloud seeding results.

---

### 5. **Way Forward**

1. **Develop a National Framework for Weather Modification:**

   * Establish **standards, protocols, and environmental safeguards** for cloud seeding.
   * Coordinate through MoES, IMD, IITM, and State agencies.

2. **Scientific Calibration and Monitoring:**

   * Expand radar coverage and use **numerical weather models** to target seedable clouds more accurately.
   * Use **before‚Äìafter analysis** to assess rainfall enhancement.

3. **Seasonal and Regional Targeting:**

   * Restrict seeding to **humid monsoon months** when clouds have adequate moisture.
   * Focus on **drought-prone regions** (e.g., Marathwada, Rayalaseema, Bundelkhand).

4. **Strengthen Research Collaborations:**

   * Build synergy between **IITs, IITM, IMD**, and **international agencies (e.g., WMO)** for long-term cloud physics research.

5. **Environmental and Cost Assessment:**

   * Replace **silver iodide** with safer alternatives like **calcium chloride** or **sodium chloride**.
   * Evaluate **cost-benefit ratios** before scaling up.

6. **Public Communication and Transparency:**

   * Clearly communicate that cloud seeding **is not guaranteed rainfall**, but a probabilistic enhancement tool.
   * Involve local communities and farmers in participatory planning.

---

### üß≠ **Conclusion**

Delhi‚Äôs 2025 cloud seeding trials mark a **symbolic and scientific milestone** ‚Äî revisiting a technique that blends **meteorology, chemistry, and engineering**. Though the trials yielded limited results, they highlight the **potential and limitations** of artificial rainmaking in India‚Äôs diverse climatic conditions.

As experiences from CAIPEEX show, cloud seeding can work **under the right meteorological conditions**, but it is **no silver bullet** for pollution or drought. A **scientifically guided, transparent, and environmentally safe** approach is essential to make cloud seeding a meaningful tool in India‚Äôs **climate adaptation and environmental management strategy**.

# What are the challenges with the High Seas Treaty?
## The High Seas Treaty (BBNJ Agreement): A Milestone for Global Ocean Governance

---

### 1. **Basic Understanding**

The **High Seas Treaty**, formally known as the **Biodiversity Beyond National Jurisdiction (BBNJ) Agreement**, is a landmark international treaty adopted under the **United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea (UNCLOS, 1982)**.

It addresses a major legal gap: **how to govern and protect marine biodiversity** in the **‚Äúhigh seas‚Äù**, which lie **beyond any single nation‚Äôs jurisdiction** ‚Äî roughly **two-thirds of the world‚Äôs ocean area** and **nearly half of the Earth‚Äôs surface**.

Until now, the high seas were largely **unregulated**, governed only by loose frameworks under UNCLOS. This led to problems such as:

* **Overfishing**
* **Marine pollution**
* **Deep-sea mining**
* **Climate change impacts**
* **Unregulated genetic resource extraction (‚Äúbiopiracy‚Äù)**

The **High Seas Treaty** aims to ensure that **conservation and sustainable use** of marine biodiversity become binding global priorities through clear rules, equitable benefit-sharing, and environmental oversight.

---

### 2. **The News**

* **Event:** Ratification of the **High Seas Treaty (BBNJ Agreement)** by **over 60 countries**.
* **Date:** September 2025.
* **Implementation:** Will come **into force in January 2026**.
* **Purpose:** To **protect, preserve, and sustainably use marine biodiversity** beyond national jurisdictions.

**Key Features of the Treaty:**

| Focus Area                                    | Description                                                                                                                                |
| --------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Marine Genetic Resources (MGRs)**           | Recognised as the **‚Äúcommon heritage of humankind.‚Äù** Ensures **fair and equitable sharing of benefits** (both monetary and non-monetary). |
| **Area-Based Management Tools (ABMTs)**       | Mechanisms like **Marine Protected Areas (MPAs)** to conserve vulnerable ecosystems and improve climate resilience.                        |
| **Environmental Impact Assessments (EIAs)**   | Mandatory **science-based EIAs** for any human activity in high seas that could have transboundary or cumulative environmental effects.    |
| **Capacity Building and Technology Transfer** | Support for **developing countries** through funding, training, and scientific collaboration.                                              |

**Timeline of the Treaty‚Äôs Evolution:**

* **2004:** UNGA forms ad-hoc working group to address biodiversity beyond national jurisdiction.
* **2011:** Agreement on four key areas (MGRs, ABMTs, EIAs, Capacity Building & Tech Transfer).
* **2018‚Äì2023:** Four rounds of **Intergovernmental Conferences (IGCs)**.
* **March 2023:** Negotiations concluded successfully.
* **June 2023:** Treaty adopted.
* **September 2025:** Ratified by 60+ nations (threshold met).
* **January 2026:** Set to enter into force.

---

### 3. **Government Interventions & International Initiatives**

#### **Global Framework**

* **UNCLOS (1982):** The foundational ‚Äúconstitution of the oceans,‚Äù but it lacked specific provisions for biodiversity conservation in areas beyond national jurisdiction.
* **Convention on Biological Diversity (CBD, 1992):** Focuses on biodiversity within national boundaries ‚Äî the BBNJ extends this globally.
* **International Seabed Authority (ISA):** Regulates seabed mining beyond national boundaries (distinct but complementary to BBNJ).
* **Regional Fisheries Management Organisations (RFMOs):** Manage fishing activities, but lacked environmental mandates.
* **Paris Agreement (2015):** Links ocean protection with climate change mitigation.

#### **India‚Äôs Position**

* India is a **party to UNCLOS** and supports **equitable access to marine resources** under the **common heritage of humankind** principle.
* India has expressed interest in exploring **marine genetic resources** for pharmaceuticals and biotechnology.
* The treaty aligns with **India‚Äôs Blue Economy policy** and **Deep Ocean Mission**, aimed at sustainable ocean resource utilisation.

---

### 4. **Mains Relevance**

**Relevant Papers:**

* *GS Paper 3 ‚Äì Environment, Biodiversity, Climate Change, and International Treaties.*
* *GS Paper 2 ‚Äì Global Governance and International Relations.*

#### **Issues and Challenges**

1. **Tension Between Two Principles:**

   * **Common Heritage of Humankind** vs. **Freedom of the High Seas**.
   * The former seeks **equitable sharing and conservation**, while the latter permits **free exploitation and navigation**.
   * The treaty‚Äôs partial application of the ‚Äúcommon heritage‚Äù concept leaves **legal ambiguity**.

2. **Governance of Marine Genetic Resources (MGRs):**

   * Risk of **‚Äúbiopiracy‚Äù** ‚Äî developed nations exploiting MGRs for pharmaceuticals or cosmetics without benefit-sharing.
   * The treaty‚Äôs framework lacks clarity on **how benefits (monetary and non-monetary)** will be calculated or distributed.

3. **Non-Participation of Major Powers:**

   * **U.S., China, and Russia** have not ratified the treaty.
   * Their absence may **limit global enforcement** and funding.

4. **Institutional Overlaps:**

   * Coordination needed with **ISA**, **RFMOs**, and other marine bodies to avoid legal conflicts or fragmented governance.

5. **Implementation and Monitoring:**

   * Establishing MPAs, tracking EIA compliance, and monitoring illegal activities across vast high seas will be **technically and financially challenging**.

#### **Impacts and Significance**

* **Environmental:** Protection of fragile marine ecosystems and enhancement of **climate resilience**.
* **Economic:** Ensures fair access to genetic resources, preventing monopolisation by a few countries.
* **Geopolitical:** Strengthens multilateralism and the **Global South‚Äôs voice** in ocean governance.
* **Scientific:** Promotes collaborative research, capacity building, and technology transfer.

#### **Ethical & Governance Aspects**

* Reflects the **‚Äúplanetary commons‚Äù approach**, emphasizing shared stewardship of global resources.
* Raises questions on **equity, justice, and inclusivity** in global environmental governance.

---

### 5. **Way Forward**

1. **Ensure Universal Ratification:**

   * Diplomatic outreach to bring **major powers (U.S., China, Russia)** onboard.
   * Incentivize participation through global partnerships and funding mechanisms.

2. **Operationalise Benefit-Sharing Mechanisms:**

   * Define **transparent procedures** for MGR access and revenue distribution.
   * Use a **Global Benefit-Sharing Fund** to support developing countries‚Äô marine conservation efforts.

3. **Strengthen Monitoring and Compliance:**

   * Establish a **High Seas Authority** under the UN to monitor activities, enforce EIAs, and penalize violations.
   * Integrate **satellite and AI-based tracking** for marine pollution and illegal fishing.

4. **Link Ocean and Climate Governance:**

   * Include high seas protection in **Nationally Determined Contributions (NDCs)** under the Paris Agreement.
   * Develop **blue carbon credits** through MPA conservation.

5. **Capacity Building for Developing Nations:**

   * Facilitate **technology transfer**, training, and marine research partnerships.
   * Support oceanographic institutions in the Global South to ensure equitable participation in marine science.

6. **Dynamic Marine Protected Areas (MPAs):**

   * Regularly assess ecological changes and adapt MPA boundaries accordingly.
   * Combine **scientific and indigenous knowledge** for effective management.

---

### üß≠ **Conclusion**

The **High Seas Treaty (BBNJ Agreement)** marks a **historic step in global environmental diplomacy**, filling a 40-year gap in international ocean law. By treating marine genetic resources as the **common heritage of humankind** and introducing binding obligations on **biodiversity protection, EIAs, and equitable benefit-sharing**, it sets the stage for a more sustainable ocean governance model.

However, its success will depend on **universal ratification, scientific integrity, and equitable implementation**. For India and other developing nations, it offers a chance to shape a **just and inclusive blue economy**, ensuring that the oceans ‚Äî humanity‚Äôs largest shared resource ‚Äî are protected not just for profit, but for posterity.
