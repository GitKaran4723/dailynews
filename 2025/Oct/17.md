# Trump and MEA at odds over Russian oil imports
## India‚ÄìU.S. Differences Over Russian Oil Imports

### 1. **Basic Understanding**

* **Background on Russian Oil Imports:**
  After the **Russia‚ÄìUkraine war (2022)**, Western countries imposed **economic sanctions** on Moscow, including restrictions on its **crude oil exports**.
  India, however, continued purchasing **discounted Russian oil** to meet its domestic energy needs, citing **energy security and market pragmatism**.

* **India‚Äôs Position:**

  * India imports about **85% of its crude oil needs**, making **supply diversification vital**.
  * The government‚Äôs stance: *India will buy oil from wherever it gets the best deal for its people.*
  * India‚Äôs purchases are **legal**, as it does not recognize unilateral Western sanctions that are not UN-mandated.

* **U.S. Position:**

  * The U.S. views India‚Äôs continued purchase of Russian oil as **undermining sanctions** aimed at isolating Moscow.
  * Under President **Donald Trump (2025)**, Washington has imposed **penalty tariffs** on certain Indian products, signaling its frustration.

---

### 2. **The News**

* **What Happened:**

  * U.S. President **Donald Trump** claimed he had been *‚Äúassured‚Äù* by PM **Narendra Modi** that India would **stop buying Russian oil**.
  * The **Ministry of External Affairs (MEA)** refuted this, saying **no such conversation** had taken place recently.

* **When & Where:**

  * The statements came in **early November 2025**, days after a phone call between the two leaders that focused on the **Gaza Peace Plan** and **India‚ÄìU.S. trade relations**.

* **Key Developments:**

  * The **MEA spokesperson Randhir Jaiswal** clarified that the two leaders did not discuss the oil issue, emphasizing that India is **‚Äúdiversifying energy sources‚Äù** based on market conditions.
  * Despite India‚Äôs stance of non-alignment, **data showed a 45% drop** in Russian oil imports by Indian public sector units (June‚ÄìSeptember 2025).
  * The issue of Russian oil imports is **holding up bilateral trade negotiations** between the two countries.

---

### 3. **Government Interventions & Initiatives**

* **India‚Äôs Energy Policy:**

  * **Diversification:** India sources oil from the Middle East, U.S., Africa, and Russia to prevent overdependence on any single region.
  * **Strategic Petroleum Reserves:** Expanding national reserves to cushion against global price shocks.
  * **Renewable Transition:** Target of **50% energy from non-fossil sources by 2030**, reducing long-term import dependence.

* **India‚Äôs Diplomatic Balancing:**

  * Maintains strategic autonomy under the **‚Äúmulti-alignment‚Äù** policy ‚Äî balancing ties with **Russia (defense and energy)** and **the U.S. (trade and technology)**.
  * India continues to participate in **Western-led initiatives** like the **IMEC** and **Quad**, while retaining strong cooperation with **Russia in defense and nuclear energy** (e.g., Kudankulam).

* **U.S. Actions:**

  * Imposed **penalty tariffs** on Indian goods as a form of pressure.
  * Seeks to align India more closely with the **G7 oil price cap mechanism**, which India has not officially joined.

---

### 4. **Mains Relevance**

#### **a) Issues & Challenges**

* **Strategic Autonomy vs. U.S. Pressure:**

  * The U.S. expects India, as a strategic partner, to align with its sanctions policy.
  * India asserts its **right to independent foreign policy** guided by **national interest**.

* **Energy Security Dilemma:**

  * India‚Äôs economy and inflation are highly sensitive to **global crude oil prices**.
  * Discounted Russian oil has helped India **control fuel inflation** and **reduce import bills**.

* **Trade Negotiations Impact:**

  * U.S. trade pressure may delay progress in the **India‚ÄìU.S. Free Trade Agreement (FTA)** talks.

* **Geopolitical Balancing:**

  * India‚Äôs **strategic ties with Russia (defense, energy)** vs. **strategic convergence with the U.S. (Indo-Pacific, technology)** make this a tight diplomatic ropewalk.

#### **b) Impacts**

* **Economic:**

  * Reduced Russian imports could increase India‚Äôs **oil import bills**.
  * Penalty tariffs from the U.S. may affect Indian exporters, especially in **steel, textiles, and chemicals**.

* **Strategic:**

  * Strained ties could undermine cooperation in **defense technology**, **semiconductors**, and **clean energy initiatives** under the **iCET framework**.

* **Diplomatic:**

  * India risks appearing inconsistent if it reduces imports under pressure, yet must also manage **perception as a responsible global actor**.

---

### 5. **Way Forward**

1. **Reaffirm Strategic Autonomy:**

   * India must continue articulating its **independent energy choices** while assuring partners of its commitment to global stability.

2. **Intensify Energy Diversification:**

   * Expand long-term oil and LNG contracts with **Gulf countries, the U.S., and Africa** to reduce exposure to geopolitical volatility.

3. **Advance Renewable Transition:**

   * Fast-track the **National Hydrogen Mission** and **biofuel initiatives** to cut fossil dependence.

4. **Engage U.S. Diplomatically:**

   * Use **iCET**, **Quad**, and **Trade Policy Forum** dialogues to mitigate trade friction and highlight India‚Äôs broader partnership role.

5. **Data Transparency:**

   * Regular publication of energy import data can strengthen India‚Äôs narrative of **market-based decision-making** rather than political alignment.

6. **Strategic Communication:**

   * Maintain consistent messaging ‚Äî balancing **global expectations** with **domestic economic imperatives** ‚Äî to avoid misinterpretations like the recent Trump claim.

# Russia backs AMCA, offers to make Su-57 jets in India
## Russia‚Äôs Offer to Support India‚Äôs AMCA Project through Su-57 Collaboration

### 1. **Basic Understanding**

* **India‚ÄìRussia Defence Partnership:**

  * India and Russia share a **70-year-old strategic relationship**, with defense cooperation as its **core pillar**.
  * Russia has been India‚Äôs **largest arms supplier**, accounting for nearly **45‚Äì50%** of India‚Äôs current military inventory.
  * The partnership has evolved from **buyer-seller** to **co-development and co-production**, including projects like:

    * **BrahMos Missile System (joint venture)**
    * **T-90 and AK-203 rifles production**
    * **S-400 Triumf air defence system** supply

* **Advanced Medium Combat Aircraft (AMCA):**

  * A **fifth-generation multirole stealth fighter** being developed by India‚Äôs **Aeronautical Development Agency (ADA)** and **Hindustan Aeronautics Limited (HAL)**.
  * Designed to replace older fighters like Mirage-2000 and MiG-29.
  * Focus areas: **stealth technology, supercruise, advanced avionics, and indigenous engines (under DRDO‚Äôs Ghatak project).**

* **Russia‚Äôs Su-57 (Felon):**

  * Russia‚Äôs **fifth-generation stealth fighter**, capable of supersonic flight, advanced radar evasion, and heavy payload carriage.
  * Entered service in 2020, manufactured by **Sukhoi** and **United Aircraft Corporation (UAC)**.
  * Previously, India and Russia had cooperated on a derivative, the **FGFA (Fifth Generation Fighter Aircraft)** project, which India withdrew from in 2018 due to cost and technology concerns.

---

### 2. **The News**

* **What Happened:**

  * Russia reaffirmed its **commitment to deepen defence ties** with India and expressed readiness to support the **AMCA programme**.
  * Russian Ambassador **Denis Alipov** said Moscow is willing to assist via **local production of Su-57 fighters** in India.

* **When & Where:**

  * Announced on **Thursday (November 2025)** at a public event marking Indo-Russian defence cooperation.

* **Key Highlights:**

---

### 3. **Government Interventions & Initiatives**

  * **Intergovernmental Commission on Military and Military-Technical Cooperation (IRIGC-M&MTC)** governs bilateral defence projects.
  * **Defence Technology and Industrial Base Cooperation Agreement (DTIBCA)** under discussion for deeper industrial collaboration.
  * Regular joint exercises: **INDRA (Army, Navy, Air Force)**.

* **Make in India in Defence:**

  * The AMCA is part of India‚Äôs drive to achieve **70% indigenisation in defence production** by 2027.
  * India has already signed multiple **Transfer of Technology (ToT)** agreements with Russia, notably for **T-90 tanks** and **Sukhoi-30MKI**.
  * India is pushing for **local assembly** of next-generation aircraft to boost domestic aerospace capabilities.

* **Russia‚Äôs Role in India‚Äôs Defence Modernisation:**

  * Supplier of **nuclear submarine (INS Chakra)**, **S-400 systems**, and **aircraft engines (AL-31FP)**.
  * Willing to share **Su-57 design know-how** and integrate **Indian sensors, avionics, and weapon systems** for AMCA synergy.

---

### 4. **Mains Relevance**

#### **a) Issues & Challenges**

* **Balancing Strategic Partners:**

  * India faces diplomatic pressure from the **U.S. (CAATSA sanctions risk)** while maintaining deep defence ties with Russia.
  * The U.S. promotes alternatives like **F-21 (Lockheed Martin)** and **Rafale-M**, seeking to reduce Russian dependence.

#### **b) Impacts**

* **Diplomatic:**

  * Reaffirms India‚Äôs **multi-alignment approach**, maintaining ties with both **Russia and the West** amid global realignments.

---

### 5. **Way Forward**

1. **Strategic Autonomy in Partnerships:**

2. **Co-Development with Conditions:**

# FSSAI says only WHO-approved products can be labelled as ORS
## FSSAI Bans Misuse of the Term ‚ÄúORS‚Äù in Food Products

### 1. **Basic Understanding**

* **Oral Rehydration Salts (ORS):**

  * A **scientifically formulated mixture of glucose and electrolytes** (sodium, potassium, chloride, citrate) used for treating **dehydration due to diarrhoea or heat stress**.
  * The **World Health Organisation (WHO)** and **UNICEF** standard formula (per litre of water):

    * Sodium chloride ‚Äì 2.6 g
    * Glucose (anhydrous) ‚Äì 13.5 g
    * Potassium chloride ‚Äì 1.5 g
    * Trisodium citrate dihydrate ‚Äì 2.9 g
  * The ratio of **glucose to sodium** and **osmolarity (~245 mOsm/L)** is critical for proper absorption.

* **Misuse in the Market:**

  * Several **fruit-based or sugar-rich drinks** were marketed as ‚ÄúORS‚Äù or ‚Äúenergy rehydration solutions‚Äù, misleading consumers.
  * These products, often containing **excess sugar** and **insufficient electrolytes**, can **worsen dehydration**, especially in children.

* **Regulatory Body:**

  * **Food Safety and Standards Authority of India (FSSAI)** ‚Äî established under the *Food Safety and Standards Act, 2006* ‚Äî regulates and enforces food safety, labeling, and consumer protection.

---

### 2. **The News**

* **What Happened:**

  * The **FSSAI issued an order** prohibiting the use of the terms **‚ÄòOral Rehydration Salts‚Äô or ‚ÄòORS‚Äô** in **any food product or beverage** unless it **exactly matches the WHO-recommended formula**.
  * The order came on **Tuesday (November 2025)** and applies **with immediate effect**.

* **Why:**

  * Triggered by the efforts of **Dr. Sivaranjani Santosh**, a **Hyderabad-based paediatrician**, who fought for nearly a decade against **misleading marketing** of sugar-laden drinks as ORS.
  * The FSSAI found that many products falsely labeled as ORS were **deceptive and potentially harmful**.

* **Key Provisions of the Order:**

  * **Withdrawal of earlier permissions** (July 14, 2022, and February 2, 2024) that allowed ‚ÄúORS‚Äù to appear in brand names with disclaimers.
  * **Strict compliance**: Only formulations **matching WHO standards** can be labeled as ORS.
  * **Legal enforcement:** Any violation will be treated as **misbranding and misleading advertising** under the *Food Safety and Standards Act, 2006*.

* **FSSAI Clarification (Wednesday):**

  * The use of ‚ÄòORS‚Äô on any **fruit-based, non-carbonated, or ready-to-drink beverage** constitutes a **violation**.
  * Such labeling **misleads consumers** and **violates Sections 3 and 24** of the *Food Safety and Standards (Packaging and Labelling) Regulations*.

---

### 3. **Government Interventions & Initiatives**

* **Regulatory Enforcement:**

  * The *Food Safety and Standards Act, 2006* empowers the FSSAI to prevent **false or misleading advertisements** (Section 24) and ensure **accurate health claims**.
  * Under the Act, using misleading health-related terms (like ‚ÄúORS‚Äù) can result in **penalties, license suspension, or prosecution**.

* **Consumer Protection:**

  * The **Consumer Protection Act, 2019** also classifies deceptive labeling as **unfair trade practice**.
  * Coordination between **FSSAI**, **Drug Controller General of India (DCGI)**, and **state food safety authorities** strengthens cross-regulatory oversight.

* **Awareness Drives:**

  * The Ministry of Health and FSSAI have periodically issued advisories warning against **non-WHO-approved rehydration drinks**.
  * The **Eat Right India movement** promotes **truthful labeling and public health safety**.

---

### 4. **Mains Relevance**

#### **a) Issues & Challenges**

* **Public Health Risk:**

  * Misleading ‚ÄúORS‚Äù drinks can **delay medical treatment** during dehydration or diarrhoea in children, leading to **life-threatening complications**.

* **Regulatory Gaps:**

  * Prior permissions (2022, 2024) allowing ‚ÄúORS‚Äù branding with disclaimers created **loopholes** exploited by beverage companies.

* **Corporate Responsibility:**

  * Food corporations prioritize **branding and profit** over **consumer safety and scientific accuracy**.

* **Legal Enforcement Challenges:**

  * Implementation and monitoring across **India‚Äôs diverse FMCG market** remain difficult due to **fragmented jurisdiction** and **resource limitations**.

#### **b) Impacts**

* **Consumer Protection:**

  * Prevents misleading labeling and ensures **public trust in healthcare-related products**.

* **Health Outcomes:**

  * Ensures only **scientifically validated ORS formulations** are sold, improving treatment outcomes in dehydration cases.

* **Industry Impact:**

  * Beverage companies will need to **rebrand or reformulate** products, leading to short-term disruptions but improved labeling standards long-term.

* **Legal Precedent:**

  * Reinforces accountability in **functional food and health supplement marketing**, possibly influencing **energy drink** and **electrolyte water** markets too.

---

### 5. **Way Forward**

1. **Strict Market Surveillance:**

   * Strengthen post-market enforcement by **FSSAI‚Äôs regional inspectors** to prevent relabeling violations.

2. **Consumer Awareness Campaigns:**

   * Use public campaigns to educate families about the **difference between WHO-ORS and sugary drinks**.

3. **Clear Labeling Standards:**

   * Mandate **distinct packaging colors and QR-coded verification** for genuine WHO-compliant ORS products.

4. **Industry Accountability:**

   * Require beverage manufacturers to undergo **pre-approval testing and certification** before claiming health benefits.

5. **Medical Collaboration:**

   * Partner with **Indian Academy of Paediatrics (IAP)** and **medical NGOs** for awareness drives in schools, rural clinics, and public health centers.

6. **Digital Monitoring:**

   * Create an **online complaint portal** under FSSAI for reporting false ORS claims or misleading advertisements.

# Ensure safeguards for India‚Äôs carbon market
## Decoupling Growth from Environmental Harm: India‚Äôs Carbon Market and the Justice Challenge

### 1. **Basic Understanding**

* **The Growth‚ÄìEnvironment Paradox:**
  The industrial revolution‚Äìdriven model of growth has propelled humanity beyond **planetary boundaries** ‚Äî particularly in climate change, biodiversity loss, and land-use change.

  * Developed nations achieved prosperity through **carbon-intensive growth**, but this model is **unsustainable** for the planet.
  * For developing countries like India, **‚Äúdegrowth‚Äù** (reducing output to cut emissions) is neither fair nor feasible, as they still battle **poverty, hunger, and development deficits**.
  * The alternative is **‚Äúdecoupling‚Äù** ‚Äî sustaining growth while reducing environmental impact through **clean energy, circular economy, and green technologies.**

* **Decoupling Explained:**

  * **Absolute decoupling:** Economic growth increases while total environmental impact falls.
  * **Relative decoupling:** Growth rises faster than emissions, but both still increase.
  * India‚Äôs policy focus is on achieving **absolute decoupling** through clean energy, sustainable agriculture, and ecosystem restoration.

* **Examples from India:**

  * **Solar energy** expansion (world‚Äôs 5th largest solar capacity).
  * **Micro-irrigation and climate-smart farming** to conserve water and soil health.
  * **Renewable energy transition** under Nationally Determined Contributions (NDCs) ‚Äî 50% non-fossil energy capacity by 2030.

---

### 2. **The News**

* **Focus:** The article highlights the **promise and perils of carbon markets** as a tool for achieving green growth without injustice.
* **What Happened:**

  * India is developing its **Carbon Credit Trading Scheme (CCTS)**, under which emission-intensive sectors will have benchmarks and voluntary offset opportunities.
  * A **national registry and trading platform** will oversee carbon credit generation, trade, and retirement.
  * Draft methodologies exist for **biomass, compressed biogas, and low-emission rice cultivation.**
  * However, India‚Äôs agricultural carbon projects remain underdeveloped ‚Äî only **4 of 64 projects** listed under Verra are registered.
* **Global Lessons:**

  * The **Northern Kenya Rangelands Carbon Project** and **Lake Turkana Wind Power Project** illustrate how top-down, poorly regulated carbon projects can **harm local land rights, exclude communities, and replicate colonial resource control.**
  * These failures serve as a **warning for India**, where customary land rights and marginalised farmer communities could face similar risks if safeguards are weak.

---

### 3. **Government Interventions & Initiatives**

* **India‚Äôs Carbon Credit Trading Scheme (CCTS):**

  * Launched under the **Energy Conservation (Amendment) Act, 2022**.
  * Overseen by the **Bureau of Energy Efficiency (BEE)** and the **Ministry of Power**.
  * Objective: Create a **domestic carbon market** that rewards emission reductions and offsets.
  * Two key components:

    1. **Compliance mechanism** for industrial sectors.
    2. **Voluntary carbon market** for agriculture, forestry, and renewables.

* **Other Climate & Sustainability Efforts:**

  * **National Action Plan on Climate Change (NAPCC)** ‚Äì includes missions on solar energy, energy efficiency, and sustainable agriculture.
  * **Green Credit Programme (2023)** ‚Äì under the Environment Ministry, encourages voluntary pro-environmental activities.
  * **India‚Äôs Updated NDC (2022):**

    * Reduce emission intensity by **45% by 2030** (from 2005 levels).
    * Achieve **net zero by 2070.**

* **Global Frameworks:**

  * **Paris Agreement (Article 6):** Allows carbon trading across nations to meet emission goals.
  * **REDD+** (Reducing Emissions from Deforestation and Forest Degradation) under UNFCCC ‚Äì promotes forest carbon credits.

---

### 4. **Mains Relevance**

#### **a) Issues & Challenges**

* **Environmental Justice Concerns:**

  * Carbon markets can **reproduce colonial patterns of land control** ‚Äî where corporations or foreign investors profit, while local communities bear costs.
  * Projects risk excluding **marginalised farmers, tribals, and forest dwellers**, especially where land titles are unclear.

* **Weak Safeguards:**

  * India‚Äôs CCTS lacks explicit provisions for **Free, Prior and Informed Consent (FPIC)** and **benefit-sharing mechanisms.**
  * Developers are not required to disclose revenue-sharing or involve local stakeholders transparently.

* **Measurement & Credibility Issues:**

  * Disputes over how much carbon is actually sequestered or avoided (e.g., flawed soil carbon measurement in Kenya).
  * Verification delays and data opacity reduce market trust.

* **Power Asymmetries:**

  * Small farmers often lack awareness or bargaining power to negotiate fair contracts with carbon credit intermediaries.

* **Regulatory Trade-Off:**

  * Overregulation could discourage investment; underregulation risks exploitation ‚Äî a delicate balance must be struck.

#### **b) Impacts**

* **Economic:**

  * Carbon markets can create a **new revenue stream** for farmers and local communities.
  * May attract **foreign climate finance** into India‚Äôs rural economy.

* **Social:**

  * Without equity safeguards, carbon projects may **alienate communities**, **disrupt commons**, and **worsen caste and class divides.**

* **Environmental:**

  * If poorly implemented, projects could **monocrop plantations** or **exclude biodiversity**, undermining genuine ecological goals.

* **Governance:**

  * Calls for institutional capacity to balance **market growth with ethical oversight.**

---

### 5. **Way Forward**

1. **Institutionalise FPIC (Free, Prior and Informed Consent):**

   * Make community consent **mandatory** before any land-based carbon project.

2. **Formalise Benefit-Sharing:**

   * Establish a **minimum revenue-sharing ratio** (e.g., 60% to community beneficiaries).
   * All contracts should be **publicly disclosed** on the carbon registry.

3. **Independent Verification & Transparency:**

   * Strengthen **MRV (Monitoring, Reporting, Verification)** protocols using **satellite data and blockchain** for credibility.

4. **Decentralised Governance:**

   * Involve **Panchayati Raj Institutions, Forest Rights Committees, and local NGOs** in project design and oversight.

5. **Adaptive Regulation:**

   * Create a **‚Äúlight but firm‚Äù regulatory framework** ‚Äî reducing red tape but enforcing accountability.

6. **Community Capacity-Building:**

   * Provide **training and technical assistance** to small farmers and tribal groups to engage meaningfully in carbon trading.

7. **Learning from Global Models:**

   * Incorporate lessons from Kenya, Peru, and Brazil on **community-led carbon projects** rooted in equity and transparency.

8. **Integrate with Development Goals:**

   * Align carbon credit projects with **SDG 13 (Climate Action)**, **SDG 1 (No Poverty)**, and **SDG 15 (Life on Land)** for holistic outcomes.

---

### üå± **Conclusion**

India stands at a crucial juncture ‚Äî capable of leading the **Global South‚Äôs transition** toward green growth. But achieving this requires not only **carbon efficiency**, but also **climate justice**. The carbon market must evolve into a tool for **empowerment, not exploitation** ‚Äî one that protects land rights, ensures fair distribution of benefits, and builds **trust-based, community-led climate action**.

# Restoring fiscal space for the States
## India‚Äôs Post-GST Fiscal Landscape and the Debate on States‚Äô Fiscal Autonomy

### 1. **Basic Understanding**

* **Goods and Services Tax (GST):**

  * Introduced in **July 2017** under the **101st Constitutional Amendment**, GST subsumed multiple indirect taxes (Central excise, VAT, service tax, etc.) into a **unified destination-based tax**.
  * GST is jointly administered by the **Centre and the States** through the **GST Council**, as per **Article 279A**.
  * The reform aimed to simplify taxation, curb cascading effects, and boost revenue through a **common market**.

* **Compensation Mechanism:**

  * To reassure States (who lost some fiscal powers), the Centre guaranteed **compensation for 5 years (2017‚Äì2022)** for any **revenue shortfall below 14% annual growth**.
  * This was funded by the **GST Compensation Cess** on luxury and sin goods.
  * As of **2025**, this cess has now been **abolished and merged with regular GST**, signaling the **end of the compensation era**.

* **Why the Debate Now:**

  * The **latest GST slab restructuring** promises **‚Çπ2 lakh crore** in tax benefits to consumers.
  * However, **States fear** revenue losses and **erosion of fiscal autonomy**, as the GST Council decisions are dominated by the Centre.
  * Some States demand a **revised fiscal sharing formula** to restore ‚Äú**cooperative federalism**.‚Äù

---

### 2. **The News**

* **What Happened:**

  * The **GST Council‚Äôs new restructuring of tax slabs** aims to simplify rates and stimulate consumption.
  * The **abolition of the compensation cess** marks a major structural shift in India‚Äôs tax architecture.
  * The Centre expects higher demand and indirect revenue growth to offset losses, but **States remain skeptical**.

* **Key Concerns of States:**

  * Lack of **transparent estimation** of the expected revenue loss.
  * Decline in **fiscal autonomy** and heavy dependence on Central transfers.
  * Growing asymmetry between **taxation powers (centralised)** and **expenditure responsibilities (decentralised)**.
  * Unequal flow of grants and **non-shareable cess/surcharge revenues** retained by the Centre.

* **Recent Trends:**

  * **Cess and surcharge collections** (non-shareable) reached ‚Çπ4.23 lakh crore (BE 2025‚Äì26).
  * Despite Finance Commission recommendations, **actual devolution** as a % of gross tax revenue is declining.
  * **Central transfers** form **44% of States‚Äô revenue receipts**, ranging from **72% in Bihar** to **20% in Haryana**, reflecting fiscal imbalance.

---

### 3. **Government Interventions & Institutional Framework**

* **Constitutional Provisions:**

  * **Article 246 & 246A:** Define taxation powers; GST gives concurrent taxing powers to both Centre and States.
  * **Articles 268‚Äì293:** Outline financial relations and distribution of revenue.
  * **Article 280:** Provides for the **Finance Commission (FC)** to recommend devolution and grants.

* **Evolution of Tax Sharing:**

  * **80th Amendment (2000):** Introduced *global sharing* of central taxes instead of sharing individual taxes.
  * **Finance Commissions:**

    * 11th FC (2000‚Äì05): 29.5% share to States.
    * 12th FC (2005‚Äì10): 30.5%.
    * 13th FC (2010‚Äì15): 32%.
    * 14th FC (2015‚Äì20): 42% (revised to 41% post-2019 due to J&K reorganisation).
  * However, actual devolution has fallen due to **rising cesses and surcharges**, which are **excluded** from the divisible pool.

* **GST Council‚Äôs Role:**

  * Functions as a **federal fiscal body**, but the **Centre holds one-third voting power**, often decisive.
  * Responsible for **tax rates, exemptions, and procedural rules** ‚Äî shifting practical fiscal control to the Union.

* **Post-GST Expenditure Pattern:**

  * Pre-GST: Centre spent 47%, States 53%.
  * Post-GST: Centre 48%, States 52%.
  * Yet, States‚Äô spending obligations (law & order, health, education, agriculture, local governance) remain higher.

---

### 4. **Mains Relevance**

#### **a) Issues & Challenges**

* **Erosion of Fiscal Autonomy:**
  GST has **centralised indirect taxation**, reducing States‚Äô power to design fiscal policy suited to local needs.

* **Dependence on Centre:**
  Central transfers and CSS (Centrally Sponsored Schemes) have increased, leading to **vertical imbalance**.

* **Cess and Surcharge Proliferation:**

  * These levies are **outside the divisible pool**, limiting States‚Äô share in central revenues.
  * Funds are often used to finance **Central schemes**, increasing discretion and politicisation.

* **Disparity Among States:**

  * Progressive States (Tamil Nadu, Maharashtra, Karnataka) argue they are **penalised** for better performance.
  * High-dependence States (Bihar, UP, MP) face **structural deficits** and low fiscal capacity.
