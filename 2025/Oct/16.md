# Trade deficit widens 93% in Sept. as services slump
## India‚Äôs Trade Deficit Widens Sharply in September 2025

### 1. **Basic Understanding**

* **Trade Balance:**
  The **trade balance** represents the difference between a country‚Äôs **exports** (goods and services sold abroad) and **imports** (goods and services purchased from abroad).

  * **Trade Surplus:** Exports > Imports.
  * **Trade Deficit:** Imports > Exports.
  * Persistent trade deficits can weaken the domestic currency, affect forex reserves, and reflect underlying structural issues in competitiveness.

* **India‚Äôs Export Structure:**

  * **Goods (Merchandise):** Petroleum products, engineering goods, gems & jewellery, chemicals, pharma, textiles, etc.
  * **Services:** IT & software, business process outsourcing (BPO), financial and professional services ‚Äî traditionally India‚Äôs strength.

* **Why Trade Deficits Matter:**

  * A growing deficit increases pressure on the **current account** and **exchange rate**.
  * However, if driven by imports of capital goods or energy inputs, it may reflect **investment-driven growth**, not weakness.

---

### 2. **The News**

* **What Happened:**
  India‚Äôs **trade deficit nearly doubled** in **September 2025**, growing **93% year-on-year** to **$16.6 billion**, from **$8.6 billion** in September 2024.

  * **Exports (Total):** $67.2 billion ‚Äî up **0.8%**.
  * **Imports (Total):** $83.8 billion ‚Äî up **11.3%**.

* **Monthly vs Half-Yearly Trends:**

  * While September saw a sharp spike in deficit, the **overall April‚ÄìSeptember 2025** trade deficit actually **narrowed by 2.3%**, indicating an improved cumulative performance.
  * This shows that **the September surge may be temporary** ‚Äî driven by specific import spikes rather than structural deterioration.

* **Breakdown:**

  * **Goods Exports:** $36.4 billion ‚Äî up **6.7%** year-on-year, despite new U.S. tariffs.
  * **Services Exports:** $30.8 billion ‚Äî down **5.5%**, marking a key drag on total export growth.
  * **Exports to the U.S.:**

    * Grew **13.4% cumulatively** (Apr‚ÄìSep 2025) compared to the same period last year.
    * However, declined steadily in recent months ‚Äî from **$8.8 billion (May)** to **$5.5 billion (September)** ‚Äî partly due to tariff impacts and weaker U.S. demand.

* **Interpretation:**

  * The short-term deterioration stems mainly from **rising import bills** (energy, electronics, gold) and a **slowdown in global services demand**, rather than a collapse in manufacturing exports.

---

### 3. **Government Interventions & Policy Context**

* **Trade Policy Measures:**

  * **Foreign Trade Policy (2023‚Äì28):** Focuses on expanding India‚Äôs exports to **$2 trillion by 2030**, with digitised approvals and trade facilitation.
  * **Production-Linked Incentive (PLI) schemes** for electronics, auto, textiles, and solar equipment are designed to **boost export-oriented manufacturing.**
  * **FTAs (Free Trade Agreements)** under negotiation with the U.K., EU, and GCC to improve market access.

* **Energy Imports:**

  * India imports over **85% of its crude oil**; fluctuations in **Brent crude prices** significantly affect the import bill.
  * Post-U.S. sanctions pressure on Russian oil and volatile West Asian geopolitics (e.g., Gaza conflict) may have temporarily spiked energy import costs.

* **Rupee and Currency Management:**

  * RBI interventions and **foreign exchange reserves (~$650 billion)** are cushioning volatility.
  * Depreciation of the rupee can make exports more competitive but raises import costs, complicating the deficit equation.

* **Service Export Dynamics:**

  * India‚Äôs IT sector faces **global slowdown**, **AI-led automation**, and **geopolitical decoupling**, reducing external demand.
  * The government is pushing to **diversify service exports** into fintech, healthcare, and education tech sectors.

---

### 4. **Mains Relevance**

#### **a) Issues & Challenges**

* **Dependence on Imports:**

  * Heavy reliance on imported crude, coal, and electronics keeps India vulnerable to **external price shocks**.
  * Low domestic value addition in manufacturing limits export competitiveness.

* **Services Sector Slowdown:**

  * Global demand for IT and business services has softened due to slower economic growth in the U.S. and EU.

* **Tariff Impact:**

  * The **50% U.S. tariff** on Indian goods (effective August 2025) has begun to hurt export momentum.
  * India‚Äôs export basket remains **concentrated** ‚Äî highly dependent on a few countries and sectors.

* **Widening Asymmetry:**

  * Imports growing faster than exports signals rising domestic consumption and investment demand ‚Äî but also structural weaknesses in **domestic manufacturing capacity**.

#### **b) Impacts**

* **Economic:**

  * May put pressure on the **current account deficit (CAD)**, though India‚Äôs large forex reserves mitigate risk.
  * Weak services exports could impact overall GDP growth, given their strong contribution to India‚Äôs external earnings.

* **Fiscal:**

  * Higher import bills could strain the fiscal balance through **fuel subsidies** or reduced excise collections.

* **Currency:**

  * Persistent deficit could **weaken the rupee** in the medium term, making imports costlier.

* **Geopolitical:**

  * Tariffs by the U.S. and a shifting global trade order underline the need for **diversified export markets** and resilient supply chains.

---

### 5. **Way Forward**

1. **Diversify Export Markets & Products:**

   * Deepen trade with **Latin America, Africa, and ASEAN**, reducing dependence on the U.S. and EU.
   * Encourage export of **value-added goods** like electronics, EV components, and pharmaceuticals.

2. **Boost Services Exports Beyond IT:**

   * Promote **tourism, healthcare, education, and fintech services**, using India‚Äôs digital infrastructure.

3. **Strengthen Manufacturing Competitiveness:**

   * Accelerate PLI implementation and **ease of doing business** to make India a global manufacturing hub.

4. **Stabilise Energy Imports:**

   * Increase **strategic petroleum reserves**, expand **renewable capacity**, and encourage **domestic oil exploration**.

5. **Leverage FTAs:**

   * Finalise and operationalise FTAs with **EU, U.K., and GCC** to improve tariff access and supply chain integration.

6. **Enhance Export Credit & Logistics Efficiency:**

   * Improve access to credit for MSME exporters and reduce logistics costs through the **PM Gati Shakti** initiative.

7. **Monitor Currency & Inflation Dynamics:**

   * Calibrated RBI interventions to maintain **exchange rate stability** and control **imported inflation**.

---

### üß≠ **Conclusion**

India‚Äôs widening trade deficit in September 2025 is **a short-term imbalance**, reflecting rising import demand amid uneven export recovery. While goods exports show resilience, the **services slowdown and tariff shocks** underscore structural vulnerabilities.

To sustain external stability, India must focus on **diversifying exports, strengthening manufacturing, and insulating itself from global shocks** ‚Äî all while maintaining a **balanced, sustainable trade policy** aligned with long-term growth and resilience.

# Refugees, infiltrators 
## Refugees vs Infiltrators: The Challenge of a Humane and Consistent Refugee Policy in India

### 1. **Basic Understanding**

* **Refugee:**
  A refugee is someone who has been forced to flee their country because of **persecution, war, or violence**, and who cannot return home safely.

  * Defined under the **1951 UN Refugee Convention** and its **1967 Protocol**, which outline the principle of **non-refoulement** ‚Äî refugees should not be returned to a country where they face serious threats to their life or freedom.
  * India is **not a signatory** to either the 1951 Convention or the 1967 Protocol.

* **Infiltrator / Illegal Migrant:**

  * As per the **Citizenship Act, 1955**, an ‚Äúillegal migrant‚Äù is a foreigner who enters India without valid documents, or stays beyond the permitted period of visa/residence.
  * In political and administrative parlance, ‚Äúinfiltrator‚Äù is often used for such persons, though it carries a **security connotation** suggesting intentional and unlawful entry.

* **India‚Äôs Current Legal Framework (as of 2025):**

  * India does **not have a specific refugee law**; it treats all foreign nationals under general immigration statutes.
  * **Earlier laws:**

    * *Foreigners Act, 1946*
    * *Registration of Foreigners Act, 1939*
    * *Passport (Entry into India) Act, 1920*
  * **From April 2025**, these were replaced by the **Immigration and Foreigners Act (IFA)**, which also subsumed the *Immigration (Carriers‚Äô Liability) Act, 2000*, thereby **streamlining immigration control** but **not addressing refugee rights**.

---

### 2. **The News**

* **Context:**
  Union Home Minister **Amit Shah** recently emphasised the need to **distinguish between refugees and infiltrators** ‚Äî a statement that revives the debate on India‚Äôs approach toward asylum seekers and undocumented migrants.

* **Current Situation:**

  * As of **June 2023**, India hosted **over 2.11 lakh refugees/persons of concern** ‚Äî including Tibetans, Sri Lankan Tamils, Afghans, Rohingya, and others from Africa and West Asia.
  * However, in the absence of a uniform policy, **different groups are treated under different yardsticks.**

* **Recent Developments:**

  * A **government notification (October 2025)** exempted **undocumented or overstaying Sri Lankan Tamil refugees** (who arrived before January 9, 2015) from penal provisions of the Immigration and Foreigners Act ‚Äî provided they are registered.
  * Similar exemptions were given to certain other groups, aligning with the **discretionary humanitarian relief approach**.
  * The **Citizenship (Amendment) Act, 2019 (CAA)**, however, remains **selective**, extending fast-track citizenship only to **non-Muslim minorities** (Hindus, Sikhs, Buddhists, Jains, Parsis, Christians) from **Pakistan, Bangladesh, and Afghanistan**.

* **Key Concern:**
  The absence of an **objective refugee determination system** leaves wide scope for **arbitrary or politicised classification**, often based on **religious, ethnic, or geopolitical considerations** rather than humanitarian ones.

---

### 3. **Government Interventions & Institutional Mechanisms**

* **Existing Legal Instruments:**

  * **Citizenship Act, 1955** ‚Äì Governs citizenship acquisition; defines ‚Äúillegal migrants.‚Äù
  * **Passports Act, 1967** ‚Äì Governs travel documentation.
  * **Immigration and Foreigners Act, 2025** ‚Äì Governs entry, stay, and deportation of all foreign nationals.
  * **Exemptions and Ad Hoc Policies:**

    * *2014 Tibetan Rehabilitation Policy* for ~63,000 Tibetans.
    * *Exemption for Sri Lankan Tamils (2025)*.
    * *Ad hoc humanitarian protection* for Afghans and some Myanmar nationals.

* **Administrative Arrangements:**

  * The **Ministry of Home Affairs (MHA)** and **Bureau of Immigration (BoI)** manage registration and deportation processes.
  * The **UNHCR (United Nations High Commissioner for Refugees)** operates in India, but only with limited recognition ‚Äî handling primarily **non-neighbouring-country asylum-seekers** (e.g., from Afghanistan, Africa).

* **Absence of Refugee Law:**

  * India relies on **executive discretion** instead of statutory protection.
  * Refugees are thus often at risk of being declared **illegal migrants** and detained/deported under existing laws.

---

### 4. **Mains Relevance**

#### **a) Issues & Challenges**

* **Legal Vacuum:**

  * Without a refugee law, India lacks a **consistent and transparent mechanism** for identifying and protecting refugees.
  * Distinction between ‚Äúrefugee‚Äù and ‚Äúillegal migrant‚Äù is left to **administrative discretion**, enabling **inconsistencies and bias**.

* **Religious and Geopolitical Bias:**

  * CAA‚Äôs religion-based exclusions have raised questions of **constitutional morality (Article 14)** and **non-discrimination**.
  * Groups like **Rohingya Muslims and Sri Lankan Tamils** remain unprotected despite clear humanitarian needs.

* **Human Rights Concerns:**

  * Refugees without documentation risk **detention, harassment, and deportation**, violating the principle of **non-refoulement**, even though India upholds it in practice.

* **Federal and Administrative Challenges:**

  * Refugee management often involves **State-level responsibilities** (housing, rationing, policing), leading to uneven implementation.

* **Security vs. Humanitarian Balance:**

  * Refugees are sometimes viewed through a **national security lens**, especially in sensitive border States, complicating humanitarian response.

#### **b) Impacts**

* **Domestic Governance:**

  * Ambiguity causes administrative delays, inconsistent treatment, and judicial burden.

* **Humanitarian:**

  * Lack of legal recognition limits refugees‚Äô access to **education, healthcare, and employment.**

* **Diplomatic:**

  * Selective refugee policies can strain relations with **neighbouring countries** and **international bodies**.

* **Social Fabric:**

  * Local resentment in host communities may rise without clear policies on integration and welfare.

---

### 5. **Way Forward**

1. **Adopt a Comprehensive Refugee Law:**

   * Enact a national **Refugee and Asylum Act**, defining refugees, rights, and procedures for status determination.
   * Include safeguards against arbitrary detention and deportation.

2. **Institutionalise a Refugee Commission:**

   * Establish an **independent Refugee Status Determination (RSD)** body involving MHA, MEA, UNHCR, and civil society representatives.

3. **Adopt Uniform Humanitarian Standards:**

   * Replace ad hoc measures with a **standard protection framework** applicable across all groups.

4. **Ensure Non-Discrimination:**

   * Refugee policy should align with **constitutional principles (Articles 14 & 21)** and **international humanitarian norms**, avoiding religion-based exclusions.

5. **State‚ÄìCentre Coordination:**

   * Develop clear guidelines for **State governments** on managing refugee camps, welfare, and local integration.

6. **Capacity Building:**

   * Train border and immigration officials to **differentiate refugees from infiltrators** based on objective humanitarian and legal criteria.

7. **Regional Cooperation:**

   * Initiate a **South Asian Refugee Framework** under **SAARC or BIMSTEC**, to handle cross-border displacement collectively.

8. **Data and Transparency:**

   * Maintain a **national refugee registry** to track inflows, status, and demographic patterns without compromising privacy or dignity.

---

### üïäÔ∏è **Conclusion**

India‚Äôs challenge lies not in its lack of compassion, but in the **absence of codified compassion**. While the Home Minister‚Äôs call to distinguish refugees from infiltrators is legitimate, the lack of an objective legal framework makes such differentiation prone to misuse.

A humane, non-discriminatory, and transparent **refugee protection regime** ‚Äî grounded in India‚Äôs constitutional ethos and humanitarian tradition ‚Äî is essential to ensure that **security concerns do not eclipse compassion**, and that India remains a responsible moral leader in the region.

# Evolution, revolution
## Nobel Prize in Economics 2025: The Theory of Creative Destruction and Its Modern Relevance

### 1. **Basic Understanding**

* **The 2025 Nobel Laureates:**

  * **Philippe Aghion**, **Peter Howitt**, and **Joel Mokyr** jointly received the **Nobel Prize in Economic Sciences** for their contributions to understanding **innovation-driven economic growth**.
  * Their work, spanning historical, theoretical, and mathematical dimensions, explains why **human progress accelerated dramatically** over the last two centuries.

* **Key Idea ‚Äì Creative Destruction:**

  * Originates from **Joseph Schumpeter (1942)**, who described capitalism as an **evolutionary process** where new innovations **continuously destroy old technologies and firms**, creating new industries.
  * This process is both **creative** (fostering innovation, productivity, and growth) and **destructive** (displacing outdated systems and workers).

* **Endogenous Growth Theory:**

  * Developed by **Aghion and Howitt (1992)**, it formalised Schumpeter‚Äôs concept using mathematics.
  * It argues that **innovation, education, and research**‚Äîgenerated *within* the economy‚Äîare the key drivers of **long-term growth**, rather than external forces (like capital accumulation).
  * This theory assumes:

    * Competitive markets.
    * Strong intellectual property rights.
    * State as an *enabler* (not a planner).
    * Openness to trade and global knowledge flows.

* **Joel Mokyr‚Äôs Contribution:**

  * Mokyr provides the **historical and cultural foundation** for modern growth, tracing innovation to the **Age of Enlightenment**, scientific curiosity, and the spread of knowledge networks.
  * His central question: *Why did modern economic growth begin in the West and not elsewhere?*

---

### 2. **The News**

* **Announcement:**

  * The **2025 Nobel Prize in Economic Sciences** was awarded for advancing understanding of **innovation as the engine of economic progress** through the **‚Äúcreative destruction‚Äù and ‚Äúendogenous growth‚Äù frameworks.**

* **Timing & Context:**

  * The award coincides with growing challenges to the **liberal, open-market capitalism** that these models assume.
  * In today‚Äôs world:

    * The **U.S. under President Donald Trump** has become **protectionist**, using trade and technology as political tools.
    * **China‚Äôs state-led innovation model** challenges the belief that only free markets can produce technological progress.
  * Hence, the Nobel recognition appears as a **reminder of the fragility of liberal economic ideals** amid rising nationalism and techno-protectionism.

---

### 3. **Government Interventions & Initiatives**

* **Schumpeterian Model vs. State-Led Models:**

  * **Western Model:**

    * Innovation driven by **entrepreneurial competition** and **private R&D**.
    * State acts as a facilitator ‚Äî through funding education, IP protection, and basic research.
  * **Eastern/State-Led Model:**

    * **China**: Direct state involvement in innovation; targeted industrial policies, state-backed tech firms (Huawei, BYD).
    * **India:** Hybrid model ‚Äî state support via **Make in India**, **Atmanirbhar Bharat**, and **PLI schemes**, but innovation mainly private-led.

* **Indian Context:**

  * **Innovation Policy:** National Research Foundation (NRF), Startup India, and Digital Public Infrastructure (like UPI, Aadhaar).
  * **Industrial Policy:** Production-linked incentives (PLIs) promote innovation-led manufacturing.
  * India‚Äôs growth increasingly reflects **state-enabled creative destruction** ‚Äî e.g., fintech replacing cash economies, renewable energy replacing coal.

* **Global Institutional Roles:**

  * **World Bank**, **WTO**, and **OECD** historically supported liberal trade frameworks that foster innovation diffusion.
  * These frameworks are now under strain due to **geopolitical rivalries**, **AI protectionism**, and **tech sovereignty** movements.

---

### 4. **Mains Relevance**

#### **a) Issues & Challenges**

* **Erosion of Open Competition:**

  * The Schumpeterian ideal assumes **free markets and scientific openness**, but global trade and knowledge flows are now **politicised**.

* **Rise of Protectionism:**

  * U.S. and EU‚Äôs **industrial policies** (e.g., CHIPS Act, Inflation Reduction Act) mirror **China‚Äôs state capitalism**, blurring ideological lines.

* **Geopolitics of Innovation:**

  * Innovation is no longer purely economic; it is strategic ‚Äî involving **AI, semiconductors, and space technology**, often restricted by export controls.

* **Exclusion and Inequality:**

  * The creative destruction model **assumes trickle-down effects** of innovation, but automation and digitisation have deepened inequality and job displacement.

* **Institutional Fragility:**

  * Democracies face declining investment in **public research**, while populist politics undermines **scientific autonomy and global cooperation**.

#### **b) Impacts**

* **Economic:**

  * Innovation remains key to productivity growth, but excessive market concentration by Big Tech contradicts the **competitive innovation** ideal.

* **Political:**

  * The Nobel‚Äôs recognition of liberal innovation theory reaffirms the **value of institutional freedoms** ‚Äî democracy, open science, and global collaboration ‚Äî for sustained progress.

* **Global Order:**

  * The ongoing tension between **state capitalism (China)** and **market capitalism (U.S. & EU)** defines the next phase of global economic evolution.

---

### 5. **Way Forward**

1. **Reassert the Role of the State as an Enabler of Innovation:**

   * The state should not retreat from industrial strategy but must ensure **transparency, inclusivity, and global cooperation** in innovation policy.

2. **Promote Open Science and Global Knowledge Sharing:**

   * Avoid the weaponisation of technology; sustain cross-border collaboration in AI, biotech, and clean energy.

3. **Redefine Competition Policy:**

   * Regulate **Big Tech monopolies** to restore the Schumpeterian cycle of competition-driven innovation.

4. **Balance Innovation with Equity:**

   * Complement creative destruction with **social safety nets**, **reskilling programs**, and **inclusive innovation ecosystems**.

5. **Encourage Public‚ÄìPrivate R&D Partnerships:**

   * Boost government investment in frontier research through **universities, startups, and incubators**.

6. **India‚Äôs Path:**

   * Leverage demographic dividend and **digital infrastructure** to foster **grassroots innovation**.
   * Integrate **state-led strategic direction** with **market-based dynamism** ‚Äî creating an ‚ÄúIndian model‚Äù of *creative inclusion*.

---

### üß† **Conclusion**

The 2025 Nobel Prize in Economics is both a **celebration and a caution** ‚Äî celebrating the power of innovation to drive growth, while cautioning against the erosion of the liberal, open systems that made such innovation possible.

In an era of **tech nationalism, fragmented globalization, and rising inequalities**, the message is clear: sustaining creativity requires **free institutions, scientific openness, and balanced state-market synergy.**

For countries like India, the challenge ‚Äî and opportunity ‚Äî lies in **harmonising creative destruction with social inclusion**, making innovation not just a force of growth, but a force of justice.

# The ‚Äòcritical factor‚Äô in India‚Äôs clean energy ambitions
## India‚Äôs Critical Minerals Strategy: Powering the Green Energy Transition

### 1. **Basic Understanding**

* **Critical Minerals:**
  Critical minerals are those essential for economic and national security, particularly for emerging technologies such as **electric vehicles (EVs)**, **renewable energy systems**, and **semiconductors**, but whose supply chains are **vulnerable** due to geographical concentration or geopolitical risks.

* **Examples Relevant to India‚Äôs Green Transition:**

  * **Lithium, Cobalt, Nickel** ‚Üí Batteries for EVs and grid storage.
  * **Rare Earth Elements (REEs)** ‚Üí Components for wind turbines, electronics, and defence applications.
  * **Graphite, Copper, Manganese** ‚Üí Conductors and renewable infrastructure.

* **Global Context:**

  * China dominates **REE production (60%)** and **processing (85%)**, making it a near-monopoly.
  * Western economies (U.S., EU, Japan) and India are now pursuing **critical mineral security strategies** to reduce dependence.

* **India‚Äôs Energy Targets:**

  * **500 GW renewable capacity by 2030.**
  * **Net zero by 2070.**
  * To achieve these, India needs secure access to minerals vital for clean technologies.

---

### 2. **The News**

India is accelerating efforts to secure **critical minerals** as part of its **green energy and Atmanirbhar Bharat vision**. With **import dependence near 100%** for lithium, cobalt, and nickel, and **90%+** for rare earths, the government is moving on three fronts:

1. **Domestic exploration and mining**,
2. **Global partnerships through KABIL**, and
3. **Recycling and circular economy frameworks.**

The **National Critical Mineral Mission (NCMM)** and recent **policy reforms in mining laws** represent the most ambitious attempt yet to build a **resilient mineral supply chain**.

---

### 3. **Government Initiatives and Policies**

#### **A. Domestic Exploration and Production**

* **National Mineral Exploration Policy (NMEP), 2016:**

  * Encourages **private sector exploration** using advanced geophysical and geochemical methods.
  * Focus on ‚Äúdeep-seated‚Äù and critical minerals.

* **Mines and Minerals (Development and Regulation) Act, 2021 & 2023 Amendments:**

  * **Opened up exploration and mining** of critical minerals to private players.
  * Introduced **auction-based licensing**, faster clearance, and transparent bidding.

* **Recent Discoveries:**

  * **5.9 million tonnes of lithium (inferred category)** found in Reasi district, Jammu & Kashmir (2023).
  * **REE reserves** identified in Odisha, Andhra Pradesh, and Jharkhand.
  * **Graphite and Nickel deposits** in Rajasthan and Chhattisgarh under exploration.

* **National Critical Mineral Mission (NCMM), 2024:**

  * ‚Çπ34,300 crore plan to strengthen value chains in exploration, mining, refining, and recycling.
  * Integrated under the Ministry of Mines; aligned with the broader **Atmanirbhar Bharat** and **energy security strategy**.

* **Public Sector Enterprises (PSEs):**

  * **IREL (India) Ltd.** ‚Üí Expanding REE processing (neodymium, praseodymium, dysprosium).
  * **NMDC** ‚Üí Diversifying into critical minerals through Australian subsidiaries.
  * **KABIL (Khanij Bidesh India Ltd., est. 2019)** ‚Üí Joint venture of NALCO, HCL, and MECL to acquire mineral assets overseas (Argentina, Chile, Australia).

---

#### **B. Recycling and Circular Economy**

* **E-Waste (Management) Rules, 2022:**

  * Mandate **collection and recovery targets** for electronics and appliances.
  * Aim to improve formal recycling (currently only 10% of 4 million tonnes of e-waste is processed).

* **Battery Waste Management Rules, 2022:**

  * Introduce **Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR)** for manufacturers.
  * Encourage recycling of lithium, cobalt, and nickel from used batteries.

* **Urban Mining & Secondary Sources:**

  * Pilot projects in Karnataka and Maharashtra for **recovery of rare metals** from discarded electronics.
  * Proposals for **Public-Private Recycling Hubs** to scale industrial recovery.

---

#### **C. Global Collaboration**

* **KABIL‚Äôs Overseas Acquisitions:**

  * Engaged in lithium projects in **Argentina‚Äôs Catamarca province** and in **Australia**.
  * Negotiations underway with **Chile and Namibia** for cobalt and nickel assets.

* **Strategic Alliances:**

  * Part of the **Minerals Security Partnership (MSP)** with the U.S., Japan, Australia, and the EU for resilient supply chains.
  * **India‚ÄìAustralia Critical Minerals Partnership (2022):** Cooperation in exploration, processing, and joint R&D.

---

### 4. **Mains Relevance**

#### **a) Issues & Challenges**

* **Import Dependence:**
  Nearly 100% for key minerals ‚Äî a major vulnerability for the EV and renewable sectors.

* **Limited Processing Capacity:**
  India contributes **<1% of global REE production** and lacks large-scale refining or separation plants.

* **Environmental & Social Concerns:**
  Mining often intersects with forest and tribal areas, raising issues of displacement and ecological degradation.

* **Regulatory Complexity:**
  Delays in clearances, multiple overlapping jurisdictions, and inconsistent enforcement deter private investment.

* **Technological Gaps:**
  Deficits in high-end processing technologies (hydrometallurgy, solvent extraction) and limited R&D in green extraction methods.

* **Global Geopolitics:**

  * Supply chains dominated by China.
  * Growing **resource nationalism** and **export restrictions** (e.g., Indonesia on nickel, China on gallium and graphite).

---

#### **b) Impacts**

* **Economic:**

  * Securing critical minerals will **reduce import bills**, **boost industrial competitiveness**, and **create green jobs**.
  * Could expand mining‚Äôs GDP share from 2.5% to 5% by 2030.

* **Strategic:**

  * Enhances **energy security** and **supply chain resilience** against global shocks.

* **Environmental:**

  * Promotes **circular economy** through recycling and resource efficiency.

* **Technological:**

  * Stimulates R&D and innovation in **battery tech, EV manufacturing, and renewables.**

---

### 5. **Way Forward**

1. **Accelerate Domestic Exploration:**

   * Fast-track auctioning and operationalisation of mineral blocks.
   * Deploy advanced remote sensing and AI-based exploration tools.

2. **Build Refining and Processing Hubs:**

   * Establish **National REE Processing Centres** with global partners (e.g., Japan, Australia).

3. **Streamline Regulatory Framework:**

   * Single-window clearance for critical mineral projects.
   * Transparent and stable policy regime for investor confidence.

4. **Boost R&D and Skill Development:**

   * Fund academic‚Äìindustry partnerships for research in extraction, substitution, and recycling technologies.

5. **Strengthen Recycling Infrastructure:**

   * Create **public‚Äìprivate recycling hubs** for batteries and electronics in industrial corridors.
   * Incentivise producers through EPR credits and carbon benefits.

6. **International Cooperation:**

   * Deepen collaboration within the **Quad** and **MSP** to diversify supply chains.
   * Use **KABIL** to strategically acquire assets in Latin America and Africa.

7. **Sustainable Mining Practices:**

   * Adopt **ESG standards** and community consultation to ensure responsible extraction.
   * Promote green mining technologies and water-efficient processes.

---

### üåè **Conclusion**

India‚Äôs clean energy revolution cannot succeed without **mineral security**. As the country accelerates toward its 2030 renewable and 2070 net-zero targets, ensuring a stable and sustainable supply of lithium, cobalt, and rare earths becomes not just an **economic necessity**, but a **strategic imperative**.

The path forward lies in **integrating domestic exploration, circular economy principles, and global partnerships** ‚Äî turning India‚Äôs mineral deficit into an opportunity for innovation, self-reliance, and global green leadership.

# The road to gender equity in courts
## Women‚Äôs Under-Representation in the Higher Judiciary: The Case for Judicial Service Reform

### 1. **Basic Understanding**

* **Context:**
  Women‚Äôs representation in India‚Äôs **higher judiciary** ‚Äî the **High Courts** and the **Supreme Court** ‚Äî continues to be alarmingly low, as revealed by the **India Justice Report (2025)**. # Why is the fiscal architecture of municipalities flawed?
## Fiscal Justice for Urban India: Reimagining Municipal Finance

### 1. **Basic Understanding**

* **Urban India‚Äôs Paradox:**

  * Indian cities generate **nearly two-thirds of national GDP**, yet **municipalities control less than 1% of tax revenue**.
  * This mismatch between **economic contribution** and **fiscal empowerment** exposes the structural weakness of **urban governance** in India.

* **Municipal Finance Defined:**

  * Refers to the **financial resources and revenue systems** that empower local governments (urban local bodies or ULBs) to deliver essential services ‚Äî sanitation, water, housing, transport, and infrastructure.
  * Revenue Sources:

    * *Own Sources:* Property tax, user charges, fees.
    * *Transfers:* Grants and shared taxes from the Centre and States.
    * *Borrowings:* Loans, bonds, and external funding.

* **Legal Framework:**

  * **74th Constitutional Amendment Act (1992)** recognised municipalities as the **third tier of governance**, envisaging **fiscal devolution** through **State Finance Commissions (SFCs)** and **intergovernmental transfers**.
  * Despite this, cities remain fiscally weak due to **centralisation of taxation powers** and **limited autonomy**.

---

### 2. **The News**

* **Context:**

  * A recent policy debate has highlighted the **fiscal marginalisation of municipalities** after the **introduction of the Goods and Services Tax (GST)** in 2017.
  * **GST subsumed key local taxes** such as octroi, entry tax, and local surcharges ‚Äî which once contributed nearly **one-fifth of municipal revenues**.
  * Compensatory transfers have bypassed city governments, making municipalities **dependent on state and central schemes**.

* **Current Scenario:**

  * Municipalities now rely heavily on **intergovernmental transfers, loans, and grants**, with limited ability to raise revenues.
  * Urban local bodies are caught in a **‚Äúresponsibility without resources‚Äù** dilemma: expected to deliver urban services, climate resilience, and infrastructure without fiscal power.

* **Municipal Bonds Issue:**

  * Promoted by NITI Aayog and urban missions (AMRUT, Smart Cities) as tools for raising funds.
  * However, **low credibility** due to flawed **credit assessment frameworks**, which undervalue grants and transfers.
  * Bond ratings often ignore **governance quality** and **citizen participation**, focusing narrowly on property tax collection.

---

### 3. **Government Interventions & Policy Mechanisms**

#### **A. Fiscal Architecture for Urban Governance**

* **74th Amendment Act, 1992:**

  * Mandates creation of **State Finance Commissions (SFCs)** to recommend devolution of taxes, duties, and grants to municipalities.
  * However, implementation remains inconsistent ‚Äî many States delay SFC reports or ignore recommendations.

* **Finance Commissions (Central):**

  * **13th, 14th, and 15th Finance Commissions** have recommended increased **tied and untied transfers** to local bodies.
  * **15th FC (2021‚Äì26)** allocated **‚Çπ4.36 lakh crore** to local governments, but **urban share (‚Çπ1.21 lakh crore)** remains small compared to rural allocations.

* **Urban Schemes:**

  * **AMRUT 2.0 (2021‚Äì26)**: Focus on water supply, sanitation, and urban mobility.
  * **Smart Cities Mission (2015):** Infrastructure innovation but limited fiscal decentralisation.
  * **Reform-linked Incentive Grants:** Conditional transfers tied to property tax reforms, but often unrealistic for smaller cities.

#### **B. Municipal Bonds and Urban Credit**

* First issued by **Ahmedabad Municipal Corporation (1998)**; since then, only ~30 cities have successfully floated bonds.
* Bonds backed by **government guarantees** or **project-specific revenues** (water supply, housing).
* **Creditworthiness gap:** RBI and SEBI regulations undervalue grants, while rating frameworks ignore structural dependence on higher governments.

---

### 4. **Mains Relevance**

#### **a) Issues & Challenges**

* **Post-GST Fiscal Centralisation:**

  * Cities lost around **19% of their own-source revenue**, with octroi and entry tax merged into GST.
  * Municipalities now depend on **discretionary transfers**, eroding financial predictability.

* **Erosion of Fiscal Autonomy:**

  * Despite being constitutionally recognised, municipalities lack **taxing powers** beyond property tax and user fees.
  * Property tax contributes only **20‚Äì25%** of potential urban revenue.

* **Ideological Bias:**

  * Policy institutions and credit agencies treat grants as **‚Äúnon-recurring‚Äù**, reducing cities‚Äô financial credibility.
  * Promotes the ‚Äú**user pays**‚Äù logic, which commodifies public goods and disproportionately burdens low-income residents.

* **Institutional Weakness:**

  * Poor accounting, audit delays, and lack of transparency deter investors.
  * State governments often withhold grants, violating SFC recommendations.

* **Equity and Efficiency Gap:**

  * Underfunded cities cannot deliver essential services, affecting the poor most acutely.
  * The **urban poor subsidise inefficiency** through user fees without seeing service improvements.

#### **b) Impacts**

* **Governance:** Weak financial autonomy undermines **democratic decentralisation** envisioned by the 74th Amendment.
* **Economic:** Limits cities‚Äô capacity to invest in **infrastructure, housing, and climate resilience**.
* **Social:** Exacerbates **urban inequality**, as poorer citizens bear higher relative costs for basic services.
* **Environmental:** Underfunded urban local bodies struggle to manage waste, pollution, and resilience projects.

---

### 5. **Way Forward**

#### **A. Reimagine Fiscal Federalism**

1. **Constitutionally Mandated Urban Transfers:**

   * Like the Finance Commission‚Äôs rural grants, urban municipalities should receive a **fixed share of national taxes**.
   * Amend the Constitution or operationalise Article 243Y to make **SFC recommendations binding**.

2. **Reinstate Local Revenue Sources:**

   * Revisit **post-GST compensatory mechanisms** to ensure a share of GST accrues directly to cities.
   * Cities should be allowed to levy **local cesses** or **environmental surcharges**.

3. **Recognise Grants as Entitlements:**

   * Credit frameworks (RBI, SEBI) should treat **central and state transfers as recurring revenue**, not charity.

4. **Democratise Fiscal Decisions:**

   * Citizen participation in **budget planning** and **expenditure tracking** to enhance transparency and accountability.

#### **B. Strengthen Municipal Bonds and Urban Credit**

1. **Reform Credit Rating Criteria:**

   * Include **governance performance**, **audit compliance**, and **citizen engagement** in bond ratings.

2. **Earmark GST or State Transfers as Collateral:**

   * Secure predictable revenue streams to improve borrowing credibility.

3. **Pooled Financing Mechanisms:**

   * Small and medium cities can pool resources to issue **joint municipal bonds** (as done by the Tamil Nadu Urban Development Fund).

4. **Capacity Building:**

   * Train municipal officials in **financial management**, **project structuring**, and **public‚Äìprivate partnerships (PPP)**.

#### **C. Redefine Urban Welfare Philosophy**

1. **Reject ‚ÄúUser Pays‚Äù for Basic Services:**

   * Treat **water, sanitation, and lighting** as **public goods**, not private commodities.
   * Subsidise essential services through progressive taxation.

2. **Learn from Global Models:**

   * Scandinavian countries (Denmark, Sweden, Norway) allow **local income taxes**, ensuring direct accountability.
   * Adapt lessons to India‚Äôs context by empowering cities with **predictable, untied revenues**.

3. **Urban Climate Funds:**

   * Establish city-level **Green Infrastructure Funds** using carbon credits and ESG-linked finance.

---

### üèôÔ∏è **Conclusion**

India‚Äôs cities are the engines of national prosperity ‚Äî yet they remain **fiscally stifled and structurally dependent**. The **post-GST regime** has deepened this imbalance, hollowing out local autonomy even as urban responsibilities expand.

True reform must rest on **fiscal justice and cooperative federalism** ‚Äî recognising that urban grants are **rights, not charity**, and cities are **partners, not dependents**, in national development.

India‚Äôs **urban future** will not be shaped merely by megaprojects or smart technologies, but by a **fair fiscal architecture** that empowers municipalities to plan, invest, and deliver ‚Äî as equal participants in the nation‚Äôs growth story.
