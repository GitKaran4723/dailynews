# Consider ‘ground realities’ in various States, SC tells EC
## Supreme Court on Special Intensive Revision (SIR) of Electoral Rolls

---

### 1. **Basic Understanding**

**What is Special Intensive Revision (SIR)?**

* SIR is a **detailed revision of electoral rolls** conducted by the **Election Commission of India (ECI)**.
* It involves **door-to-door enumeration**, verification of voters, deletion of duplicate/shifted names, and inclusion of eligible voters.
* It is more **rigorous** than summary revision.

**Why electoral roll revision matters?**

* Electoral rolls are the **foundation of free and fair elections** (Article 324 – powers of ECI).
* Errors can lead to:

  * Exclusion of genuine voters (disenfranchisement)
  * Inclusion of ineligible voters (electoral fraud)

**Constitutional & Legal Basis**

* **Article 324**: Superintendence, direction, and control of elections vested in ECI
* **Representation of the People Act, 1950**: Preparation and revision of electoral rolls
* **Democratic principle**: Universal adult suffrage (Article 326)

---

### 2. **The News (What happened?)**

* The **Supreme Court** asked the **Election Commission** to take a **“sympathetic view”** on requests to **extend the enumeration phase** of the SIR exercise.
* The Court emphasized considering **“ground realities”** in States.
* **Petitions from Uttar Pradesh and Kerala** argued that the enumeration was **too rushed**.
* **Kerala**:

  * Enumeration deadline extended to **Dec 18**
  * Draft voter list to be published on **Dec 23**
* **Uttar Pradesh**:

  * Deadline extended to **Dec 26**
* Petitioners questioned the **urgency**, as **UP Assembly elections are due only in 2027**.
* The Court **did not issue a mandatory order**, but **requested** the EC to take an appropriate decision.
* Hearing on the **constitutionality of SIR** scheduled for **January 6, 2026**.

---

### 3. **Key Stakeholders & Their Positions**

**Petitioners (UP & Kerala)**

* Enumeration phase is **too hasty**
* Risk of **large-scale exclusion**, especially in:

  * Migrant populations
  * Elderly, poor, urban slums
* No immediate election → no urgency

**Election Commission of India (ECI)**

* Argued that:

  * EC is already **monitoring ground realities**
  * Extensions have been given earlier
  * Courts should avoid micromanaging election processes

**Supreme Court**

* Balanced approach:

  * Did **not interfere directly**
  * Emphasized **administrative sensitivity**
  * Retained **judicial oversight** by listing constitutionality hearing

---

### 4. **Mains Relevance (GS Paper II – Polity & Governance)**

**Core Issue**: Balance between **electoral integrity** and **voter inclusion**

#### a) Democratic Concerns

* Hasty SIR can lead to:

  * Disenfranchisement → violation of democratic rights
  * Reduced trust in electoral institutions
* Particularly affects:

  * Migrant workers
  * Urban poor
  * Linguistic and regional minorities

#### b) Institutional Balance

* **Independence of ECI** vs **Judicial oversight**
* Courts traditionally avoid interfering in election schedules, but:

  * Can intervene when **constitutional rights** are at stake

#### c) Federal Dimension

* Large and diverse States like UP require:

  * Adequate time
  * Administrative capacity
* One-size-fits-all timelines ignore **State-specific realities**

#### d) Precedent Value

* Court’s use of words like **“sympathetic view”** shows:

  * Soft judicial nudging instead of hard orders
  * Respect for separation of powers

---

### 5. **Way Forward**

**For Election Commission**

* Adopt a **State-specific timeline** for SIR
* Increase:

  * Enumerator manpower
  * Use of technology (online verification + physical checks)
* Wide public awareness campaigns in local languages

**For Judiciary**

* Continue **constitutional scrutiny** without operational overreach
* Ensure electoral processes remain:

  * Inclusive
  * Transparent
  * Rights-oriented

**For Governance**

* Synchronize SIR with:

  * Migration data
  * Urbanization trends
* Regular, less disruptive revisions instead of hurried intensive drives

---

### 6. **Prelims Pointers (Quick Facts)**

* Article 324 → Powers of Election Commission
* Electoral rolls governed by → RP Act, 1950
* Universal adult suffrage → Article 326
* SIR ≠ Summary revision (more detailed & field-based)

  * **Trade in goods**
  * **Trade in services**
  * **Investment**
  * **Movement of professionals**
  * **Regulatory cooperation**

**Why CEPA matters?**

* It aims to **reduce tariffs**, **increase market access**, and **build long-term economic partnerships**.
* CEPAs are part of India’s strategy to integrate with **global value chains** and boost exports.

**India’s CEPA strategy**

* After stagnation in FTAs, India has adopted a **new-generation FTA approach** (post-2021):

  * UAE CEPA
  * Australia ECTA
  * Now **Oman CEPA**

---

* Liberalised tariffs on **77.79% of tariff lines**

# Cutting off a rural lifeline and the Directive Principles
## Replacement of MGNREGA & Constitutional Debate (Viksit Bharat–G RAM G Bill, 2025)

---

### 1. **Basic Understanding**

**What is MGNREGA?**

* Enacted in **2005** as the *Mahatma Gandhi National Rural Employment Guarantee Act*.
* It provides a **legal guarantee of 100 days of wage employment** per year to every rural household whose adult members volunteer for unskilled manual work.
* It is:

  * **Demand-driven** (work must be given if demanded)
  * **Universal** (no targeting)
  * **Rights-based** (legal entitlement, not charity)

**Constitutional foundation**

* **Article 41 (Directive Principles of State Policy)**:

  * Directs the State to make effective provision for the **right to work**, within its economic capacity.
* Dr. **B.R. Ambedkar**:

  * Called DPSPs “**instruments of instruction**” for lawmakers.
  * Essential for achieving **economic democracy**, even if non-justiciable.
* **Debate in Constituent Assembly**:

  * Socialist members (e.g., **K.T. Shah**) wanted right to work as a Fundamental Right.
  * Capitalist lobby opposed it → compromise led to inclusion in DPSPs.

**Importance of MGNREGA**

* Partial realisation of Article 41.
* Acts as:

  * A **fallback livelihood option**
  * A **wage floor** in rural labour markets
  * A tool for **women empowerment** and **social justice**

---

### 2. **The News**

* The **Union Government**, using its majority in the Lok Sabha, has introduced a Bill to **replace MGNREGA** with the **Viksit Bharat – G RAM G Bill, 2025**.
* Critics argue that:

  * The Bill **changes the core nature** of MGNREGA.
  * It is an **“adhikar chori” (rights theft)** Bill.
  * It amounts to an **assault on the Constitution**, especially:

    * Article 41
    * Federal principles
    * Social justice commitments
* Demand raised that the Bill must be sent to a **Parliamentary Standing Committee** for scrutiny.

---

### 3. **Political & Historical Context**

**Role of the Left (2004–05)**

* MGNREGA became possible due to:

  * **UPA government without majority** (2004)
  * **Outside support of Left parties**, especially CPI(M)
* Left parties:

  * Fought elections independently.
  * Actively shaped the final Act passed **unanimously** by Parliament in 2005.
* Result:

  * Recognition of State responsibility to ensure work.
  * Allocation of national resources for rural employment.

---

### 4. **Core Features of MGNREGA (Present Law)**

* **Demand-driven**: Employment depends on demand from households.
* **Legal guarantee**: Failure to provide work → unemployment allowance.
* **Centre–State balance**:

  * Centre bears **entire wage cost**.
  * States bear ~10% cost + implementation role.
* **Decentralisation**:

  * Panchayats identify and design works.
* **Social justice**:

  * Equal wages for men and women.
  * High participation of:

    * Women (>50%)
    * SCs (~19%)
    * STs (~18%)
* **Democratic content**:

  * Workers can opt out when better work is available and return when needed.

---

### 5. **What the New Draft Bill Changes (Critical Analysis)**

#### a) **From Demand-driven to Allocation-driven**

* Work will now depend on **“normative financial allocations”** fixed by the Centre.
* If demand exceeds allocation:

  * **No legal obligation** on the Centre to provide work.
* This **removes the right-based character**.

#### b) **Centralisation & Federalism**

* States now required to bear **40% of costs** (earlier ~10%).
* Centre controls:

  * Project design
  * Digital audits
  * Implementation norms
* States already face:

  * Fiscal stress
  * Reduced share in central taxes
* → **Direct assault on federal structure**.

#### c) **Agricultural Season Restriction**

* Prohibits work during **peak agricultural season**.
* Reality:

  * Mechanisation has reduced farm employment days.
  * Workers use MGNREGA **only when farm wages are low or work is unavailable**.
* Impact:

  * Weakens bargaining power of rural labour.
  * Benefits **big landowners**.
  * Harms women workers (already paid lower wages).

#### d) **Digital Conditionalities**

* Mandatory:

  * Aadhaar linkage
  * Online attendance
* Ground reality:

  * Poor connectivity
  * Authentication failures
* Result:

  * Exclusion of genuine workers.

#### e) **Dilution of Representation**

* Removal of SC/ST and worker representation from:

  * Advisory
  * Redress councils
* Undermines **constitutional protection of vulnerable groups**.

---

### 6. **MGNREGA as a Mirror of Rural Distress**

* Women workers often lift **up to 3,000 kg of mud/day** to meet norms.
* Yet women form the majority → shows **lack of alternatives**.
* Participation data:

  * STs: 8.6% of population → ~18% of MGNREGA workers
  * SCs: ~19% of workers
* Indicates:

  * Deep agrarian distress
  * Structural unemployment

**Funding trends**

* Since 2014:

  * Workers increased to **7.7–8.9 crore**
  * Expenditure stagnated (<0.2% of GDP)
* 2024–25:

  * 8.9 crore demanded work
  * Only 7.9 crore got work
  * **99 lakh workers turned away**
* Wage arrears up to **₹8,000 crore**
* Average workdays: **<50 days**, not 100
* Promise of **125 days** in new Bill seen as hollow without legal guarantee.

---

### 7. **Mains Relevance (GS II & GS III)**

**GS II – Polity & Constitution**

* DPSPs vs State policy
* Federalism and centralisation
* Parliamentary accountability (Standing Committees)
* Rights-based welfare vs executive discretion

**GS III – Economy & Social Justice**

* Rural employment
* Agrarian distress
* Labour markets and wage floors
* Gender and caste dimensions of development

**Ethics & Governance**

* Dignity of labour
* State responsibility towards vulnerable sections
* Procedural justice vs digital exclusion

---

### 8. **Way Forward**

* Send the Bill to a **Standing Committee** for detailed scrutiny.
* Retain:

  * Demand-driven nature
  * Legal entitlement
* Strengthen funding:

  * Link allocations to rural distress indicators.
* Use technology as an **enabler**, not a gatekeeper.
* Deepen decentralisation:

  * Restore Panchayat autonomy.
* Treat MGNREGA as:

  * A **constitutional obligation**, not a fiscal burden.

---

# A bold step amid an ambitious nuclear energy target
## Energy Consumption, Human Development & India’s Nuclear Imperative (with SHANTI Bill, 2025)

---

### 1. **Basic Understanding**

**Energy–Human Development Link**

* Human development **correlates strongly with energy consumption**.
* **Earl Cook (Scientific American, 1971)** traced stages of civilisation by **per capita energy use**:

  * **Primitive**: energy only for food
  * **Hunting**: food + home + commerce
  * **Agriculture**: adds industry & transport
  * **Industrial/Technological**: rapid rise across all sectors
  * **Digital era**: **additional electricity demand** due to data, devices, automation

**Human Development Index (HDI)**

* Composite index of **income, education, health**.
* Empirically linked to **per capita Final Energy Consumption (FEC)** → energy is an *enabler* of HDI gains.

---

### 2. **The Core Numbers & Projections (India)**

* **Target HDI**: ~**0.9** (peer group of G-20 advanced economies)
* **Energy requirement to reach HDI ~0.9** (with efficiency gains & electrification):

  * **~24,000 TWh/year** (Curr. Sci., 2022)
  * **~60% as electricity**
  * **~40% to produce hydrogen** (via electrolysers) for hard-to-abate sectors (steel, fertilisers, plastics)
* **Current generation (2023–24)**: ~**1,950 TWh**
* **Recent CAGR**: ~**4.8%**

  * At this pace → **24,000 TWh in ~4–5 decades**

**Two Complications**

1. **Decarbonisation** must happen *alongside* growth.
2. **Energy mix redesign** is essential (more electricity in FEC; less fossil fuels).

---

### 3. **Why Electrification & Decarbonisation Are Central**

* **Electricity share in FEC today**: ~**22%** → must **rise sharply**.
* Fossil-heavy mix must transition to **low-carbon sources**:

  * **Hydro, Solar, Wind, Nuclear**.
* **Hydrogen**: critical for decarbonising industry; initially electricity-intensive.

---

### 4. **Limits of Renewables & the Baseload Problem**

**Renewables—Strengths and Limits**

* **Hydro & Wind**: geographically constrained in India.
* **Solar**: land constraints due to **high population density**.
* **Intermittency**: solar/wind vary by time of day and season.

**Storage Challenge**

* Short-term storage is costly; **seasonal storage is prohibitively expensive** at scale.
* Grid reliability requires **baseload power**—steady, non-intermittent generation.

**Conclusion**

* A **decarbonised, affordable grid** needs **baseload** → **Nuclear power** is essential.

---

### 5. **India’s Nuclear Capability: What Has Been Achieved**

**Indigenous Supply Chain**

* **Fuel fabrication**, **heavy water production**, **equipment manufacturing** → indigenous.
* **Uranium**: imported (limited domestic reserves).

**Technology & Operations**

* **PHWRs (Pressurised Heavy Water Reactors)** mastered by NPCIL.
* **700 MW** is the largest PHWR design:

  * **3 units operational**
  * **4th nearing completion**
  * **2 more advanced**
* **2017 sanction**: **10 × 700 MW PHWRs** under execution.

**Safety & Waste**

* **Atomic Energy Regulatory Board (AERB)**: regulatory capacity since 1980s.
* **BARC**: reprocessing of spent fuel; waste handling expertise.

**Assessment**

* Nuclear in India is **technically feasible, affordable, and safe**.

---

### 6. **The SHANTI Bill, 2025**

**What is SHANTI?**

* **Sustainable Harnessing and Advancement of Nuclear Energy for Transforming India (SHANTI) Bill, 2025**.
* An **overarching framework** consolidating:

  * **Atomic Energy Act, 1962**
  * **Civil Liability for Nuclear Damage Act, 2010**

**Key Provisions**

* Existing **AERB deemed constituted** under the new Act.
* **Licensee bears prime responsibility** for:

  * Safety
  * Security
  * Safeguards

**Policy Signal**

* **Target**: **100 GW nuclear installed capacity by mid-century**.
* Parliament’s passage reflects **strategic commitment** to nuclear as a pillar of development.

---

### 7. **Mains Relevance (GS II & GS III)**

**GS III – Energy, Growth & Climate**

* Energy as a **binding constraint** on HDI.
* Decarbonisation pathways; **hydrogen economy**.
* Baseload vs intermittency; grid economics.

**GS II – Governance & Institutions**

* Regulatory capacity (AERB), liability framework.
* State responsibility vs licensee accountability.

**Ethics & Sustainability**

* Inter-generational equity (climate mitigation).
* Energy justice—**affordable, reliable power** for development.

---

### 8. **Balanced Critique (For Answers/Interviews)**

**Strengths**

* Realistic appraisal of energy needs.
* Indigenous nuclear capability reduces strategic dependence.
* Baseload support enables higher renewable penetration.

**Concerns**

* Capital intensity and long gestation of nuclear.
* Public perception and safety communication.
* Uranium import dependence—needs diversified sourcing.

**Way Forward**

* Parallel push: **nuclear + renewables + grids + efficiency**.
* R&D in **advanced reactors** and **non-electrolytic hydrogen** to cut electricity load.
* Transparent regulation and community engagement.

# Is the Artificial Intelligence boom a bubble?
## Is the AI Boom a Bubble or a Lasting Technological Shift?

---

### 1. **Basic Understanding**

**What is happening?**

* Global spending on **Artificial Intelligence (AI)** is projected to reach:

  * **$375 billion** this year
  * **$500 billion by 2026** (UBS estimates)
* This sharp rise in investment raises a classic economic question:

  > Is AI driven by **real productivity gains**, or by **investor hype running ahead of reality**?

**Core dilemma**

* History shows that **every major technology revolution** passes through phases of:

  * Hope → Hype → Overinvestment → Corrections → Long-term gains
* The debate is whether AI is currently in a **speculative bubble**, similar to the dot-com era, or in the **early, disorderly phase of a genuine transformation**.

---

### 2. **Understanding the “Bubble” Argument**

The idea that AI might be a bubble comes from three main concerns:

1. **Weak monetisation**

   * Many companies use AI, but **few generate clear profits** from it yet.
   * Reports (e.g., McKinsey) suggest only a small minority see meaningful AI-driven returns.

2. **Dot-com comparison**

   * Investors recall how massive enthusiasm in the late 1990s led to:

     * Overvalued firms
     * Market crash
   * Fear: AI could follow the same trajectory.

3. **Technological limits**

   * Current AI systems:

     * Excel in **narrow tasks**
     * Struggle with **general intelligence**, reliability, common sense, and factual correctness.
   * Scaling up models no longer guarantees proportional improvements.

---

### 3. **Why the Dot-com Analogy Is Incomplete**

**Dot-com was not a failure of technology**

* Though many firms collapsed financially:

  * The **Internet fundamentally reshaped society**
  * E-commerce, cloud computing, social media, digital payments all emerged later

**Key insight**

* Speculation destroys **companies**, not **technologies**.
* Out of hundreds of AI startups:

  * Most may fail financially
  * A few will survive and **reshape the world**

Thus, a market correction does not imply technological regression.

---

### 4. **Where AI Actually Stands Today**

**AI is not new**

* AI already underpins:

  * Spam filters
  * Recommendation systems
  * Search engines
  * Speech recognition and translation

**What changed after 2022?**

* The release of **ChatGPT** marked an inflection point:

  * Popularised conversational AI
  * Triggered belief in **autonomous agents** and task automation at scale

**Reality check**

* AI performs well in **narrow, well-defined domains**
* Truly autonomous, human-like intelligence:

  * Remains uncertain
  * Has no predictable timeline

---

### 5. **Mismatch Between Market Expectations and Science**

**Market expectation**

* Rapid arrival of:

  * Artificial General Intelligence (AGI)
  * Fully autonomous agents replacing human labour

**Scientific reality**

* Scaling laws are **plateauing**
* Bigger models alone are insufficient
* Progress now depends on:

  * New architectures
  * Better data
  * Alignment, safety, and reasoning breakthroughs

**Better analogy than dot-com**

* AI resembles the **search engine revolution**:

  * Early hype
  * Unclear business value
  * Gradual, cumulative breakthroughs over time

---

### 6. **Where AI’s Biggest Gains Are Likely to Come From**

**Underappreciated domains**

* Scientific research:

  * Biology (protein discovery)
  * Chemistry
  * Drug development
  * Materials science
  * Mathematical research
* These areas:

  * Offer **transformative long-term gains**
  * Do not provide **immediate commercial returns**

**Policy implication**

* Societies must decide:

  * Whether they are willing to invest in **slow-burn scientific progress**
  * Or only in short-term commercial AI applications

---

### 7. **Risk of “AI Winter” and Investment Cycles**

**Historical context**

* AI has faced **multiple AI winters**:

  * 1960s–70s
  * 1980s–90s
* Triggered when hype exceeded capability, leading to funding cuts.

**Current risk**

* If profits do not materialise soon:

  * Markets may reprice AI companies
  * Funding could slow down

**Key distinction**

* Markets reprice **companies**, not **knowledge**
* Technological progress continues even if investors retreat.

---

### 8. **Macroeconomic and Systemic Risk Perspective**

* Investment in chips and data centres ≈ **0.5% of global GDP**
* Key concern is not investor losses, but:

  * **Systemic financial instability**
  * Cascading failures in banks or markets

**Lesson from dot-com**

* Consumers and society benefited despite investor losses.
* Risk-taking is inherent in technological advancement.

---

### 9. **Efficiency, Chips, and the Economics of AI**

* More computing power generally improves AI systems.
* Current focus:

  * Innovation and scale
* Missing focus:

  * **Efficiency**
  * **Competition in chip markets**
* Improving efficiency can:

  * Reduce costs
  * Make AI economically sustainable even with lower hype.

---

### 10. **Final Assessment: Bubble or Transformation?**

**Balanced conclusion**

* The AI boom is **both**:

  * A set of **small speculative bubbles** that will burst
  * The **early, messy phase of a lasting technological shift**

**Why this is not alarming**

* Failure of weak firms cleans the ecosystem.
* Long-term gains accrue gradually across sectors.
* The key risk is not market correction, but:

  * Security failures
  * Systemic economic shocks
  * Loss of long-term research funding

---

### 11. **Way Forward (Policy & Governance Perspective)**

* Encourage **long-term AI research funding**, insulated from market volatility.
* Regulate to:

  * Prevent systemic financial risks
  * Ensure safety, reliability, and trust.
* Balance:

  * Short-term commercial applications
  * Long-term scientific and societal gains.
* Improve efficiency in:

  * Chips
  * Data centres
  * Energy use.

# Inside the DHRUV64 microprocessor
## DHRUV64 Microprocessor: India’s Indigenous Processor Push (MEITY)

---

### 1. **Basic Understanding**

**What is DHRUV64?**

* **DHRUV64** is a **fully indigenous 64-bit microprocessor** developed by **C-DAC** under the **Ministry of Electronics and Information Technology (MEITY)**.
* It is part of India’s **Microprocessor Development Programme**, aimed at reducing dependence on imported processor designs and supply chains.

**Why processors matter strategically**

* Microprocessors are the **core “brain”** of all electronic systems — telecom networks, industrial control systems, automobiles, defence electronics.
* Control over processor **design, toolchains, updates, and IP** determines:

  * Cybersecurity
  * Resilience during export controls
  * Supply-chain sovereignty

---

### 2. **Technical Nature of DHRUV64**

* **Architecture**: 64-bit, general-purpose microprocessor
* **Configuration**: Dual-core
* **Clock speed**: ~1 GHz
* **Capability implication**:

  * Powerful enough to run **modern operating systems**
  * Efficient enough for **embedded and industrial use**

**What it is NOT**

* Not a high-end consumer chip (smartphones/laptops)
* No mention of GPUs, AI accelerators, or high core counts

**Where it fits best**

* Telecom subsystems
* Industrial automation
* Routers and control systems
* Automotive electronics
* Strategic and sovereign systems where **reliability and control** matter more than raw performance

---

### 3. **India’s Processor Ecosystem (Big Picture)**

DHRUV64 is **not standalone**; it fits into a broader indigenous ecosystem:

* **SHAKTI** – IIT Madras
* **AJIT** – IIT Bombay
* **VIKRAM** – ISRO–Semiconductor Lab
* **THEJAS32 / THEJAS64** – C-DAC
* **DHRUV64** – Third DIR-V chip

**Purpose of this ecosystem**

* Strategic systems (defence, space, critical infrastructure)
* Factory control systems
* Platforms for startups, academia, and industry
* Prototyping new architectures **without foreign dependence**

**Key insight**

> Processors succeed **only when an ecosystem exists** — software, developer tools, boards, documentation, long-term supply.

---

### 4. **RISC-V and DIR-V Explained Simply**

**What is RISC-V?**

* An **open instruction set architecture (ISA)**.
* ISA = the basic “language” a processor understands (add, move, compare, jump).

**Why RISC-V matters**

* **No licence fees**
* Open standards → transparency
* Modular design → customisation for security, performance, or power efficiency
* Preferred by governments for **sovereignty**

**DIR-V (Digital India RISC-V)**

* India’s programme to build a **portfolio of RISC-V chips**.
* DHRUV64 is the **third chip** under DIR-V.

**Concern**

* MEITY has not clarified **where DHRUV64 is fabricated**, raising questions on supply-chain control.

### 5. **Critical Gaps and Unknowns in DHRUV64**

Despite the announcement, **engineering clarity is limited**. Five major gaps stand out:

#### (1) **Performance Transparency**

* No benchmarks provided
* No details on:

  * Cache size
  * Memory controller
  * I/O capabilities
  * Performance per watt

#### (2) **Manufacturing & Reliability**

* Foundry and process node not disclosed
* No data on:

  * Yields
  * Failure rates
  * Lifecycle availability
* Critical for telecom and automotive OEMs

#### (3) **Meaning of “Fully Indigenous”**

* Ambiguous:

  * Does it mean only open ISA?
  * Indigenous core design?
  * Indigenous toolchain?
  * Indigenous fabrication?
  * Indigenous ownership of IP blocks?

#### (4) **OEM & Developer Readiness**

* No clarity on:

  * Developer boards
  * OS support (Linux, RTOS)
  * Security features
  * Audit and certification
  * Government anchor procurement

#### (5) **Roadmap Uncertainty**

* Next chips announced:

  * **DHANUSH** – 1.2 GHz, quad-core (28 nm)
  * **DHANUSH+** – 2 GHz, quad-core (14/16 nm)
* Timelines, volumes, and manufacturing plans unclear
