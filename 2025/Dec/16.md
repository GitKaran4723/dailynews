# With new Bill, demand-led job scheme set to turn supply-driven
## **Viksit Bharat ‚Äî Guarantee For Rozgar and Ajeevika Mission (Gramin) Bill (VB-G RAM G): A Paradigm Shift in Rural Employment Policy**

![Image](https://www.theindiaforum.in/sites/default/files/field/image/2022/06/21/sarguja-cg-raghav-puri-1584756311-1584756311.jpeg)

![Image](https://www.studyiq.com/articles/wp-content/uploads/2023/06/21160027/Mahatma-Gandhi-National-Rural-Employment-Guarantee-Scheme-1.jpg)

![Image](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/G8mjh05bcAAUfqm.jpg)

![Image](https://ptfund.org/wp-content/uploads/2018/08/india.jpg)

---

### 1. **Basic Understanding**

**What is MGNREGA?**
The **Mahatma Gandhi National Rural Employment Guarantee Act, 2005** is a **rights-based**, **demand-driven** law that guarantees **100 days of wage employment** to every rural household on demand.

* If work is not provided within 15 days ‚Üí **unemployment allowance**
* Objective: **livelihood security**, poverty reduction, and creation of durable rural assets

**Key idea of demand-driven scheme**

* People demand work ‚Üí State is legally bound to provide it
* Budget follows demand, not the other way around

---

### 2. **The News: What is Changing?**

The Union government plans to introduce the **Viksit Bharat ‚Äî Guarantee For Rozgar and Ajeevika Mission (Gramin) Bill** in the **Lok Sabha**, replacing MGNREGA.

**Stated objective**

* Align rural employment policy with the national vision of **Viksit Bharat @2047**

This marks a **fundamental shift in philosophy**:

> From a *legal entitlement to work* ‚Üí to an *administratively allocated employment scheme*

---

### 3. **Key Features of the VB-G RAM G Bill**

#### a) **Shift from Demand-driven to Supply-driven**

| Aspect         | MGNREGA         | VB-G RAM G                            |
| -------------- | --------------- | ------------------------------------- |
| Nature         | Demand-driven   | Supply-driven                         |
| Budget         | Open-ended      | **Capped**                            |
| Work provision | On demand       | Based on **Union-decided allocation** |
| Coverage       | All rural areas | Only **Centre-notified rural areas**  |

üëâ Employment will depend on **budgetary ceilings**, not legal demand.

---

#### b) **Change in Guaranteed Workdays**

* **MGNREGA**: 100 days
* **VB-G RAM G**: **125 days**

üìå On paper, this looks like an expansion, but actual availability depends on allocation.

---

#### c) **Increased Financial Burden on States**

| Component   | MGNREGA  | VB-G RAM G |
| ----------- | -------- | ---------- |
| State share | ~10%     | **40%**    |
| Union share | Majority | Reduced    |

Under MGNREGA:

* Union paid **100% labour wages**
* Union paid **75% material costs**

Under the new Bill:

* States must mobilise much higher resources

üëâ This disproportionately affects **fiscally weaker States**.

---

### 4. **Why Is This a Major Policy Shift? (Analytical View)**

#### a) **Erosion of Rights-based Welfare**

* MGNREGA created a **justiciable right to work**
* VB-G RAM G converts it into a **scheme**, not a right
* No clarity on:

  * Unemployment allowance
  * Grievance redressal mechanisms

---

#### b) **Impact on Federalism**

* Higher State contribution without greater decision-making power
* Centre decides:

  * Budget size
  * Notified rural areas

üëâ This tilts the balance towards **centralisation**, challenging cooperative federalism.

---

#### c) **Risk of Exclusion**

* Selective notification of rural areas
* Budget caps may:

  * Exclude needy regions during distress
  * Reduce shock-absorber role during droughts, pandemics, or migration surges

---

### 5. **Arguments Likely to Be Made by the Government**

* MGNREGA allegedly became:

  * A ‚Äúconsumption subsidy‚Äù
  * A permanent welfare scheme rather than a development catalyst
* VB-G RAM G aims to:

  * Integrate **livelihoods (Ajeevika)** with skill creation
  * Focus on **productive, planned rural development**
* Predictable budgeting and fiscal discipline

---

### 6. **Concerns Raised by Experts & States**

* Loss of **automatic stabiliser** role of MGNREGA
* Poor States may:

  * Curtail implementation
  * Opt out informally
* Rural distress may go **unreported** if demand itself is not recorded
* Women and SC/ST workers, who form a large share of MGNREGA workforce, may be disproportionately affected

---

### 7. **UPSC Mains Relevance**

#### **GS-II (Governance & Federalism)**

* Rights-based legislation vs scheme-based welfare
* Fiscal federalism and Centre‚ÄìState relations

#### **GS-III (Inclusive Growth & Rural Economy)**

* Rural employment as social protection
* Impact on migration, agrarian distress, and consumption demand

#### **Ethics (GS-IV)**

* State obligation towards vulnerable populations
* Equity vs efficiency in public policy

---

### 8. **Way Forward (Balanced Suggestions)**

* Retain **demand-driven core** while improving asset quality
* Flexible budgeting with contingency provisions
* Differential State contribution based on fiscal capacity
* Clear legal safeguards for:

  * Unemployment allowance
  * Transparency & social audit
* Convergence with skilling and livelihood missions **without diluting the right to work**

---

# Why were IndiGo operations disrupted?
## **IndiGo Operational Meltdown (December 2025): What Went Wrong & What It Means for Indian Aviation**

![Image](https://www.hindustantimes.com/ht-img/img/2025/12/05/550x309/Indigo_Flight_airport_chaos_1764914066284_1764914075986.jpeg)

![Image](https://www.flyeye.io/wp-content/uploads/2024/07/DGCA-India.jpg)

![Image](https://pilotworkshop.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/04/fatigue_350.jpg)

![Image](https://image.chitra.live/api/v1/wps/2b9714c/fe73a81e-28cc-48d7-9c47-56b1c3a6c1e9/0/ANI-20251220041620-620x420.jpg)

---

### 1. **Basic Understanding**

**Why is this episode important?**
The near-collapse of IndiGo‚Äôs operations in early December is not just an airline-specific failure. It highlights **systemic weaknesses** in:

* Aviation regulation
* Human resource planning
* Labour relations
* Passenger protection
* Safety culture

It brings back the long-standing tension between **cost efficiency vs flight safety**.

---

### 2. **The News: What Happened?**

* Airline involved: **IndiGo**
* Period: **First 10 days of December 2025**
* Impact:

  * **5,000+ flights cancelled**
  * ~900 cancellations already in November
  * **12.5 lakh passengers affected**
  * ‚Çπ1,100 crore issued as refunds
  * ‚Çπ10,000 vouchers for severely impacted passengers (Dec 3‚Äì5)

**Operational chaos included**:

* Crew stranded at wrong airports
* Pilots idle without duty rosters
* Baggage reaching destinations without passengers
* Call centres crashing
* Passenger protests inside terminals

IndiGo admitted to the regulator that:

* There were **‚Äúplanning gaps‚Äù**
* Misjudgement of pilot availability under new rules
* Shortage of **65 pilots-in-command**

---

### 3. **Immediate Regulatory Response**

* Regulator: **Directorate General of Civil Aviation**
* Granted **temporary exemption** to IndiGo from new pilot duty rules
* Exemption valid till **February 10**
* Rationale: prevent further large-scale passenger disruption

---

### 4. **Backdrop: Flight Duty Time Limitation (FDTL) Rules**

This crisis revives a **decades-old safety vs productivity debate**.

#### a) **Why FDTL exists**

* To prevent **pilot fatigue**
* Fatigue reduces:

  * Alertness
  * Decision-making
  * Reaction time

---

#### b) **Triggering tragedy: Mangalore crash (2010)**

* Crash date: **May 22, 2010**
* Finding:

  * Cockpit voice recorder showed **1h40m of snoring**
  * Flight occurred during **Window of Circadian Low (2 am‚Äì6 am)**
* Lesson:

  * Night flying + sleep debt = safety hazard

---

#### c) **2011 FDTL Rules (Pilot-friendly)**

Based on:

* Nasim Zaidi Committee (2011)
* NASA fatigue studies

**Key provisions**:

* No consecutive night duties
* Night landings capped at **2 hours**
* Maximum duty time capped at **9 hours**

---

#### d) **2019 Dilution (Airline-friendly)**

* Allowed **two consecutive night duties**
* Used **vague language** on duty and flight time limits
* Pilot unions challenged it in court

---

#### e) **2024 Revised Rules (Balanced but stricter)**

Notified in January 2024:

* Weekly rest increased from **36 ‚Üí 48 hours**
* Night flying:

  * Max 2 hours landing
  * Max 8 hours flying
  * Max 10 hours duty

**Delhi High Court intervention (April 2025)**:

* Phased rollout:

  * Most clauses from **July 1**
  * Night duty clauses from **November 1**

üëâ IndiGo‚Äôs meltdown occurred **exactly after November 1**.

---

### 5. **Why Only IndiGo Collapsed?**

| Airline          | Situation                            |
| ---------------- | ------------------------------------ |
| Air India        | Excess pilots due to aircraft delays |
| Akasa / SpiceJet | Grounded aircraft, lower utilisation |
| **IndiGo**       | Tight staffing, hyper-efficiency     |

**Structural reason**:

* IndiGo‚Äôs business model:

  * Maximum aircraft utilisation
  * Minimal buffer pilots
  * Razor-thin margins

üìä Data highlights:

* Market share: **47% (pre-Covid) ‚Üí 65% now**
* Employee cost: **11% ‚Üí 8%**

üëâ Growth without proportional workforce expansion.

---

### 6. **Deeper Structural Issues**

#### a) **Labour morale & safety culture**

* Leadership‚Äìemployee disconnect
* Pilots, cabin crew, ground staff bore passenger anger
* Risk of:

  * Under-reporting incidents
  * Skipping rest to avoid penalties

---

#### b) **Pilot training & debt trap**

* Training cost:

  * IndiGo: ‚Çπ1.2 crore
  * Air India: ‚Çπ1.5 crore
* Often **50% higher than market rates**
* Comparable to foreign flight schools

**Consequences**:

* Young pilots enter decade-long debt
* Pressure to:

  * Accept fatigue
  * Avoid filing safety reports

---

#### c) **Regulatory mindset**

* Human factors still treated as:

  * Labour issues
  * Not core safety issues
* Fatigue science inadequately integrated into regulation

---

### 7. **Passenger Rights: A Missing Link**

* India‚Äôs Passenger Charter of Rights exists, but:

  * Poor awareness
  * Weak enforcement
* Need:

  * Clear, airline-specific entitlements
  * Simple, transparent dashboards (like U.S. FAA tables)
  * Real-time information on delays, refunds, baggage rules

---

### 8. **UPSC Mains Relevance**

#### **GS-II (Governance & Regulation)**

* Role of independent regulators
* Balancing consumer interest and industry viability

#### **GS-III (Infrastructure & Transport)**

* Aviation safety
* Human factors in high-risk industries
* Market concentration risks

#### **GS-IV (Ethics & Case Studies)**

* Profit vs safety
* Organisational ethics
* Employee exploitation through debt

---

### 9. **Way Forward (Policy Suggestions)**

**For airlines**

* Build staffing buffers
* Improve rostering software & forecasting
* Reduce training cost exploitation

**For regulator**

* Treat fatigue as **safety science**, not HR issue
* No blanket exemptions; use data-based flexibility
* Independent audit of pilot training practices

**For government**

* Strengthen Passenger Charter of Rights
* Improve transparency via regulator portals
* Promote competition to avoid ‚Äútoo-big-to-fail‚Äù airlines

# Does India need to upgrade its biosecurity measures?
## **Biosecurity in the Age of New-Age Biotechnology: Why India Must Upgrade Its Defences**

![Image](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/e4/Structure_of_NIAID_Integrated_Research_Facility.jpg)

![Image](https://biosafety.utk.edu/wp-content/uploads/sites/30/2019/03/Section3-considerations.png)

---

### 1. **Basic Understanding**

#### **What is Biosecurity?**

**Biosecurity** refers to the **policies, institutions, practices and technologies** designed to:

* Prevent **intentional misuse** of biological agents, toxins, or biological knowledge
* Detect, deter, and respond to **deliberate biological attacks** or bioterrorism

It spans across:

* **Human health** (pathogens, pandemics)
* **Animal health** (livestock diseases)
* **Plant & agricultural health** (crop pests, invasive species)

üëâ **Core idea**: *Protection against deliberate biological harm*.

---

#### **Biosecurity vs Biosafety (Important UPSC Distinction)**

| Aspect  | Biosafety                      | Biosecurity             |
| ------- | ------------------------------ | ----------------------- |
| Focus   | Accidental exposure or leakage | Intentional misuse      |
| Example | Lab safety protocols           | Preventing bioterrorism |
| Nature  | Technical & procedural         | Strategic & regulatory  |

üìå **Strong biosafety ‚Üí foundation for strong biosecurity**

---

### 2. **Global Context: Biological Weapons & Regulation**

After historical instances of bioweapons development, the world adopted the
**Biological Weapons Convention (1975)**

**Key features**

* Prohibits:

  * Development
  * Production
  * Stockpiling
  * Use of biological weapons
* Mandates destruction of existing stockpiles
* First treaty to ban an entire category of **Weapons of Mass Destruction**

India is a **signatory**, reinforcing its normative commitment.

---

### 3. **Why Does India Need Robust Biosecurity?**

#### a) **Geographical & Ecological Vulnerability**

* Long, porous borders
* High biodiversity
* Dense population
* High dependence on **agriculture & livestock**

üëâ Any biological attack or outbreak can scale rapidly.

---

#### b) **Threat from Non-State Actors**

* Reported preparation of **Ricin toxin** (from castor seeds) for terror use
* Shows:

  * Low-cost, high-impact nature of biological agents
  * Ease of access to biological materials

---

#### c) **Rise of New-Age Biotechnologies**

* Gene editing
* Synthetic biology
* Recombinant DNA research

These technologies:

* Improve disease control & innovation
* But also **lower entry barriers** for malicious misuse

üëâ **Dual-use dilemma**: same tools for good and harm.

---

### 4. **India‚Äôs Existing Biosecurity Architecture**

India already has **multiple institutions**, but they operate in silos.

#### **Key Institutions**

* **Department of Biotechnology**

  * Research governance
  * Lab safety frameworks

* **National Centre for Disease Control**

  * Disease surveillance
  * Outbreak response

* **Department of Animal Husbandry and Dairying**

  * Livestock biosecurity
  * Transboundary animal diseases

* **Plant Quarantine Organisation of India**

  * Controls agricultural imports/exports

* **National Disaster Management Authority**

  * Guidelines for biological disasters

---

#### **Legal Framework**

* **Environment (Protection) Act, 1986**

  * Hazardous microorganisms
  * GMOs

* **Weapons of Mass Destruction Act, 2005**

  * Criminalises biological weapons

* **Biosafety Rules, 1989**

* **2017 Guidelines** for:

  * Recombinant DNA research
  * Biocontainment

---

### 5. **India in Global Biosecurity Regimes**

* Member of:

  * **Biological Weapons Convention**
  * **Australia Group**

    * Controls exports of dual-use biological & chemical materials

---

### 6. **Gaps & Concerns**

#### a) **Lack of Unified National Biosecurity Framework**

* Multiple agencies, no single coordinating authority
* Fragmented response during crises

---

#### b) **Global Health Security Index (GHSI)**

* India ranked **66**
* Improvements in:

  * Detection & surveillance
* Decline in:

  * Response capacity
  * Emergency coordination

üëâ Detecting threats without response capacity = **false security**

---

### 7. **Risks Ahead if Biosecurity Remains Weak**

* Mass casualties
* Collapse of public health systems
* Agricultural devastation
* Economic paralysis
* National security threat equivalent to warfare

üëâ Biosecurity is no longer just a **health issue**, but a **strategic security issue**.

---

### 8. **Way Forward: What Should India Do?**

#### **1. National Biosecurity Framework**

* Central coordinating authority
* Clear roles across ministries
* Real-time information sharing

#### **2. Strengthen Response Capacity**

* Surge labs
* Rapid response teams
* Simulation & stress-testing

#### **3. Regulation of Dual-Use Research**

* Ethical oversight
* Licensing & audits
* Researcher accountability

#### **4. Integrate Biosecurity with National Security**

* Include in:

  * National Security Strategy
  * Defence & intelligence assessments

#### **5. International Cooperation**

* Intelligence sharing
* Capacity building
* Norm-setting leadership

---

### 9. **UPSC Mains Relevance**

#### **GS-II (Governance & International Relations)**

* Global conventions
* Institutional coordination
* Treaty obligations

#### **GS-III (Science, Technology & Internal Security)**

* Biotechnology risks
* Bio-terrorism
* Disaster management

#### **Ethics (GS-IV)**

* Dual-use dilemma
* Responsibility of scientists
* Precautionary principle
