# States told to ‚Äòenforce‚Äô SC‚Äôs Aravalli orders
## **Aravalli Ranges & Sustainable Mining Issue**

---

### **1. Basic Understanding**

**Aravalli Range**

* One of the **oldest fold mountain ranges in the world**.
* Extends across **Rajasthan, Haryana, and Gujarat**.
* Acts as:

  * A **climatic barrier** against desertification from the Thar Desert.
  * A **groundwater recharge zone** for North-West India.
  * A **biodiversity corridor** (forests, wildlife, minerals).

**Why mining is sensitive here**

* Rich in minerals like marble, limestone, and granite.
* Unregulated mining has caused:

  * Forest loss
  * Groundwater depletion
  * Dust pollution
  * Land degradation and desert expansion

**Judicial Background**

* The **Supreme Court of India** has repeatedly intervened to:

  * Curb illegal mining
  * Protect ecologically sensitive zones of the Aravallis
  * Apply the **Precautionary Principle** and **Sustainable Development**

---

### **2. The News (What Happened?)**

* The **Union Environment Ministry** has:

  * Issued **directions to Chief Secretaries** of Haryana, Rajasthan, and Gujarat.
  * Ordered **no new mining leases** in the Aravalli region **until a comprehensive management plan is prepared**.
* **Existing mines**:

  * Can continue **only if they strictly comply** with environmental norms.
* The Ministry has directed **Indian Council of Forestry Research and Education (ICFRE)** to:

  * Prepare a **Management Plan for Sustainable Mining**.
  * Identify:

    * **Permissible mining areas**
    * **Ecologically sensitive & conservation-critical zones**
    * **Restoration-priority areas**
* **Concern**:

  * No deadline has been fixed ‚Üí risk of delay and policy dilution.

---

### **3. Government Interventions & Institutions Involved**

**Judicial**

* Supreme Court orders banning or restricting mining in ecologically sensitive Aravalli zones.

**Executive**

* Ministry of Environment, Forest and Climate Change (MoEFCC)
* State governments (Haryana, Rajasthan, Gujarat)

**Expert Body**

* ICFRE (technical and scientific mapping & planning)

**Legal & Policy Tools**

* Environment Protection Act, 1986
* Forest Conservation Act, 1980
* Sustainable Development & Intergenerational Equity principles

---

### **4. Mains Relevance (GS Paper II & III)**

**Key Issues**

* **Environmental Governance**: Balancing development with ecological protection.
* **Centre‚ÄìState Coordination**: States issue leases, Centre frames safeguards.
* **Judicial Oversight**: Courts stepping in due to weak enforcement.

**Challenges**

* Illegal and proxy mining operations
* Political‚Äìeconomic nexus in mining
* Lack of updated ecological zoning
* Absence of strict timelines

**Impacts**

* Long-term water scarcity in NCR and western India
* Increased desertification
* Loss of carbon sinks and biodiversity
* Public health issues due to dust and pollution

**Ethics & Governance Angle**

* Conflict between **short-term economic gains** vs **long-term ecological security**
* Responsibility towards future generations

---

### **5. Way Forward**

1. **Time-bound Management Plan**

   * Fix a clear deadline for ICFRE‚Äôs report.

2. **Scientific Zoning**

   * Landscape-level mapping using GIS & satellite data.
   * Clear demarcation of:

     * No-go zones
     * Conditional mining zones
     * Restoration zones

3. **Strict Compliance Mechanism**

   * Independent environmental audits.
   * Heavy penalties and mine closures for violations.

4. **Community Participation**

   * Involve local communities in monitoring and restoration.
   * Promote alternative livelihoods (eco-tourism, forestry).

5. **Restoration & Afforestation**

   * Mandatory mine closure plans.
   * Use CAMPA funds for Aravalli restoration.

6. **Institutional Coordination**

   * Permanent Aravalli Conservation Authority (inter-state).

# The deliberate unmaking of India‚Äôs ‚Äòright to work‚Äô
## **From MGNREGA to VB-G RAM G Act, 2025: A Rights-Based Welfare Retreat**

![Image](https://www.theindiaforum.in/sites/default/files/field/image/2022/06/21/sarguja-cg-raghav-puri-1584756311-1584756311.jpeg)

![Image](https://www.pciglobal.in/wp-content/uploads/2023/05/MGNREGA-copy.jpg)

![Image](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/359875340/figure/fig1/AS%3A11431281079282224%401660626290755/MGNREGS-Performance-Across-the-Different-States-2006-21.png)

![Image](https://m.economictimes.com/thumb/msid-94925885%2Cwidth-1200%2Cheight-900%2Cresizemode-4%2Cimgsize-185550/centre-to-define-minimum-work-for-mgnrega-to-maintain-standard.jpg)

---

### **1. Basic Understanding**

**What was MGNREGA?**

* Enacted in **2005** as NREGA; renamed **MGNREGA in 2009**.
* Provided a **legal, demand-driven right to 100 days of wage employment** to rural households.
* Core principles:

  * **Right-based** (citizens could demand work).
  * **Decentralised** (Gram Panchayats played a central role).
  * **Accountability mechanisms** (social audits, compensation for delays).
* Symbolically anchored to **Gandhian ethics** of dignity through work.

**Why it mattered**

* Addressed **livelihood insecurity**, not just poverty.
* Acted as:

  * A safety net during agrarian distress.
  * A counter-cyclical tool (especially visible during **COVID-19**).
* Recognised employment as the **most dignified form of social protection**.

---

### **2. The News (What Has Changed?)**

* Parliament has enacted the **Viksit Bharat-Guarantee for Rozgar and Ajeevika Mission (Gramin) Act, 2025 (VB-G RAM G)**, replacing MGNREGA.

* Key shifts:

  1. **Demand-driven ‚Üí Supply-driven** framework.
  2. **Right to work diluted** ‚Üí Employment depends on Central allocation.
  3. **Funding ratio changed**:

     * Earlier: ~**90:10 (Centre:State)**
     * Now: **60:40**, sharply increasing States‚Äô fiscal burden.
  4. **Centralisation**:

     * Centre decides **where, how much, and whether** employment will be provided.
  5. **Symbolic break**:

     * Removal of **Mahatma Gandhi‚Äôs name**, signalling an ideological shift.

* According to **Jean Dr√®ze**, one of the architects of NREGA, these changes **amount to dismantling rather than reform**.

---

### **3. Government Interventions & Institutional Context**

**Earlier framework**

* Supported by UPA‚Äôs **rights-based welfare architecture**, along with:

  * **Right to Information Act**
  * **Forest Rights Act**
* Sought to **temper market-led growth** with enforceable social guarantees.

**Current shift**

* VB-G RAM G reflects:

  * Greater **executive discretion**.
  * Reduced **State autonomy**.
  * Welfare framed as **administrative benevolence**, not legal entitlement.

---

### **4. Mains Relevance (GS II & GS III)**

#### **A. Constitutional & Federal Issues (GS II)**

* **Right vs Discretion**

  * MGNREGA operationalised **Article 21 (Right to Life with dignity)** through work.
  * VB-G RAM G weakens enforceability ‚Üí citizens become **beneficiaries, not rights-holders**.
* **Federal Balance**

  * Increased State financial burden without commensurate fiscal support.
  * Poorer States likely to **cut employment**, deepening regional inequality.
* **Democratic Process**

  * MGNREGA: unanimous parliamentary support.
  * VB-G RAM G: passed amid walkouts, no Standing Committee scrutiny ‚Üí **democratic deficit**.

#### **B. Economic & Social Impact (GS III)**

* **Livelihood Security**

  * Rural India largely informal; limited social security.
  * Curtailing guaranteed work risks:

    * Distress migration
    * Wage suppression
    * Agrarian instability
* **Empirical Evidence**

  * MGNREGA raised rural wages, improved asset creation, enhanced resilience during crises.
  * Claims of ‚Äúunproductivity‚Äù contradicted by evidence.

#### **C. Political Economy Angle**

* Shift from **social-democratic compromise** to **market-centric governance**.
* Rights-based entitlements:

  * Decentralise power.
  * Enable citizens to make claims on the State.
* Discretionary schemes:

  * Centralise authority.
  * Risk turning welfare into **political loyalty instruments**.

---

### **5. Way Forward**

1. **Restore Rights-Based Core**

   * Employment must remain a **justiciable entitlement**, not a discretionary scheme.
2. **Rebalance Fiscal Federalism**

   * Reinstate higher Central wage support for poorer States.
3. **Strengthen Accountability**

   * Protect social audits, grievance redressal, and delay compensation.
4. **Democratic Law-Making**

   * Major welfare reforms must go through:

     * Standing Committees
     * Stakeholder consultation
5. **Integrate with Rural Transformation**

   * Use employment works for:

     * Climate resilience
     * Water conservation
     * Sustainable agriculture

---

### **1. Basic Understanding**

**What are Rare Earth Elements (REEs)?**

* Group of **17 elements** (15 lanthanides + scandium, yttrium).
* Not truly ‚Äúrare‚Äù, but **difficult to separate and refine**.
* Used in:

  * EV motors
  * Wind turbines
  * Smartphones & defence electronics

**Key Concept ‚Äì The Real Bottleneck**

* The problem is **not all REEs**, but a **small subset**:

  * **Neodymium (Nd), Praseodymium (Pr), Dysprosium (Dy)**.
* These are critical for **high-performance permanent magnets**, especially:

  * **Neodymium‚ÄìIron‚ÄìBoron (NdFeB) magnets**.
* These magnets act as **gatekeepers** of the green transition:

  * If magnet supply fails ‚Üí EVs, wind turbines, electronics slow down.

**Mining vs Refining (Very Important)**

* Mining REEs ‚â† Strategic control.
* **Chemical separation & refining** is the true chokepoint.
* Analogy:

  * Mining is like **crude oil extraction**.
  * Refining is like **petroleum processing**.
* This is why **China** dominates even when deposits exist elsewhere.

---

### **2. The News (What is India Doing?)**

* In **late-2025**, India shifted focus from **just mining** to **magnet manufacturing**.
* Government announced a **‚Çπ7,280-crore scheme** to:

  * Build an **integrated ecosystem** for
  * **6,000 tonnes/year of sintered rare earth permanent magnets**.

**Why magnets, not mines?**

* Magnets are:

  * High-value
  * High-impact
  * Directly linked to EVs, wind energy, electronics.
* Import dependence on magnets = **strategic vulnerability**.

**Policy Alignment**

* Linked with:

  * **National Critical Mineral Mission**
  * Green transition goals
  * Industrial self-reliance

---

### **3. Structural Challenges in India‚Äôs REE Supply Chain**

#### **A. Upstream: Resource & Governance Complexity**

* India‚Äôs key domestic source:

  * **Monazite-bearing beach sands**.
* Problem:

  * Monazite occurs alongside **thorium** ‚Üí linked to nuclear programme.
* Implications:

  * Tight regulation
  * Multiple agencies
  * Public sector dominance
  * High governance sensitivity

üëâ REEs in India are not just an **industrial issue**, but also a **strategic & nuclear governance issue**.

---

#### **B. Midstream: Refining & Separation Capacity**

* Exploration alone is insufficient.
* **Geological Survey of India** has projects assigned till 2031.
* Missing links:

  * Commercial-scale separation plants
  * Reliable financing
  * Regulatory clarity
  * Strong environmental enforcement

---

#### **C. Downstream: Magnet Manufacturing Viability**

* Magnet plants need:

  * Assured **long-term offtake** from:

    * EV manufacturers
    * Wind turbine makers
    * Electronics firms
* Without this:

  * Projects remain unbankable.
* Also needed:

  * **Process innovation** to reduce dependence on the most constrained elements (like Dy).

---

### **4. Mains Relevance (GS III ‚Äì Economy, Environment, Science & Tech)**

#### **A. Climate & Green Transition**

* REEs are **low-volume but high-leverage** minerals.
* Any disruption ‚Üí inflation, slowed decarbonisation.
* Future green leadership depends on:

  * **Resilient supply chains**, not just targets.

#### **B. Industrial Policy**

* India moving from:

  * Resource extraction mindset
  * To **value-chain control** mindset.
* Magnet manufacturing enables:

  * EV ecosystem
  * Wind energy manufacturing
  * Advanced electronics

#### **C. Geopolitics**

* China‚Äôs dominance is structural, not accidental.
* Diversification without:

  * Environmental safeguards
  * Community consent
    ‚Üí risks repeating extractive injustices elsewhere.

#### **D. Environmental Governance**

* REEs involve:

  * Toxic waste
  * Radioactive by-products
* Green transition without green compliance = **policy contradiction**.

---

### **5. Way Forward**

1. **Prioritise Magnets as Strategic Assets**

   * Treat NdFeB magnets like semiconductors.
2. **Build Midstream Capacity**

   * Separation & refining plants with strict environmental oversight.
3. **Assured Demand Creation**

   * Long-term procurement contracts from EV & wind sectors.
4. **Innovation & Substitution**

   * R&D to reduce Dy-intensity in magnets.
5. **Environmental & Social Credibility**

   * Waste management, coastal community engagement.
6. **Risk Distribution**

   * Avoid over-concentration in one region or firm.

# Why manufacturing has lagged in India
## **Dutch Disease, Wages, and India‚Äôs Missed Industrialisation**

---

### **1. Basic Understanding**

**India‚Äôs puzzle**

* Around the early 20th century, **India**, **China**, and **South Korea** started from **broadly comparable income and development levels**.
* Today:

  * **China & South Korea** ‚Üí strong manufacturing bases.
  * **India** ‚Üí manufacturing share in GDP **stagnant**, services dominate.

**Why manufacturing matters**

* Manufacturing:

  * Absorbs large numbers of semi-skilled workers
  * Drives productivity growth
  * Enables export competitiveness
* Weak manufacturing ‚Üí **jobless growth**, informality, inequality.

---

### **2. The Argument in the News**

* Economist **Arvind Subramanian**, in his book **A Sixth of Humanity**, uses the **Dutch disease** framework to explain India‚Äôs under-industrialisation.
* His core claim:

  * **High government salaries** drew labour away from manufacturing.
  * This raised economy-wide wages and prices.
  * Manufacturing became uncompetitive ‚Üí failed to expand.

---

### **3. What is the Dutch Disease?**

**Original idea**

* Coined after the discovery of **Groningen gas fields (1959)** in the Netherlands.
* Mechanism:

  1. Resource boom ‚Üí higher wages
  2. Currency appreciation (real exchange rate)
  3. Imports become cheaper, exports costlier
  4. Manufacturing shrinks

**Key insight**

* A **boom in one sector** can unintentionally **damage others**.

---

### **4. Applying Dutch Disease to India**

**India‚Äôs version**

* No oil or gas windfall.
* Instead:

  * Expansion of **public sector employment**
  * Relatively **high government wages**

**Transmission channel**

* Government wages ‚Üí raise overall demand.
* Domestic prices rise.
* Even without nominal currency appreciation:

  * **Real exchange rate appreciates**.
  * Imports look cheaper.
  * Domestic manufacturing loses demand.

üëâ Outcome: **Manufacturing stagnates**, services expand.

---

### **5. The Critical Question: Is This Explanation Enough?**

Here lies the deeper analytical debate.

#### **A. Policy vs Natural Windfall**

* Dutch disease was designed for **accidental windfalls**, not **democratic policy choices**.
* Government salaries are:

  * Conscious
  * Political
  * Normative (linked to welfare, dignity, state capacity)

So:

* Can we equate **public salaries** with **oil discovery**?
  ‚Üí Economically similar effects, but **politically very different causes**.

---

### **6. Technology & Induced Innovation: The Missing Link**

**Induced innovation theory**

* High wages and labour scarcity can **force firms to innovate**.

**Historical evidence**

* **John Habakkuk**:

  * Britain innovated due to land & labour scarcity.
* **Robert C. Allen**:

  * High British wages ‚Üí Industrial Revolution.
* **Daron Acemoglu**:

  * Automation raised productivity in ageing economies like:

    * Germany
    * Japan
    * South Korea

üëâ **Logic**:
High wages **should** push firms toward technology, not stagnation.

---

### **7. Then Why Didn‚Äôt Indian Manufacturing Innovate?**

This is the **core analytical puzzle**.

#### **Possible Explanations**

**1. Cheap labour trap**

* India‚Äôs abundance of labour:

  * Reduced pressure to automate.
  * Encouraged labour-intensive, low-productivity models.

**2. Weak technological ecosystems**

* Limited R&D spending.
* Poor firm‚Äìuniversity linkages.
* Risk-averse industrial culture.

**3. State‚Äìprivate sector mismatch**

* Public sector ensured wage floors.
* Private manufacturing:

  * Did not match productivity growth with wage growth.
  * Relied on informalisation instead.

**4. Services-led growth distortion**

* Software & platform firms:

  * Scale via labour abundance, not deep technology.
  * Entry-level wages stagnant since early 2000s.

Examples:

* Swiggy, Zomato, Ola, Blinkit:

  * Business model depends more on **cheap labour pools** than productivity leaps.

---

### **8. Broader Implications**

**Structural transformation stalled**

* India skipped:

  * Agriculture ‚Üí Manufacturing ‚Üí Services
* Instead:

  * Agriculture ‚Üí Services (premature deindustrialisation)

**Inequality**

* High corporate profits
* Billionaires multiply
* Wages stagnate ‚Üí unequal growth

**Political economy insight**

* Public sector wages may have protected workers.
* Private sector failed to **upgrade technology** and **share productivity gains**.

---

### **9. Way Forward**

1. **Technology-first industrial policy**

   * Incentivise automation & R&D.
