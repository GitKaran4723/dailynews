# Retail inflation moves up marginally to 0.7% in Nov.
## Retail Inflation in India ‚Äì November 2025 (CPI Analysis)

---

### 1. **Basic Understanding**

**Retail Inflation (CPI)**

* Measures price changes faced by consumers.
* Released by **Ministry of Statistics and Programme Implementation (MoSPI)**.
* Used by **RBI‚Äôs Monetary Policy Committee (MPC)** to set interest rates.
* CPI composition (approximate):

  * Food & beverages: **~46%**
  * Housing
  * Fuel & light
  * Clothing & footwear
  * Miscellaneous (health, education, transport, etc.)

**Base Effect**

* Inflation is calculated year-on-year.
* If prices were **very high last year**, current inflation may appear **artificially low**.
* This is crucial to understand current inflation trends.

---

### 2. **The News (What happened?)**

* **Retail inflation (CPI) in November 2025**: **0.7%**

  * Slightly higher than **October 2025 (0.25%)**
  * Still the **second-lowest ever** in the current CPI series.
* Inflation has **slowed in 7 of the first 8 months** of FY 2025‚Äì26.
* Trend driven mainly by:

  * **Food price contraction**
  * **Strong base effect**

---

### 3. **Component-wise Inflation Trends**

#### (a) **Food & Beverages**

* Prices **contracted by 2.8%** in November 2025.
* Comparison:

  * November 2024: **+8.2%** (high base)
  * October 2025: **‚Äì3.7%**
* Key drivers:

  * Sharp fall in prices of:

    * **Potatoes**
    * **Onions**
    * **Tomatoes**
    * **Pulses**
* Food inflation overall fell by **3.9%**.

**Exception within food**

* **Edible oils inflation**: **7.9%**

  * Driven mainly by:

    * Mustard oil
    * Coconut oil

üëâ Because food has the **highest weight**, its contraction pulled down headline CPI.

---

#### (b) **Fuel & Light**

* Inflation rose to **2.3%** in November 2025

  * October 2025: **2%**
  * November 2024: **‚Äì1.8%**
* Indicates:

  * Mild upward pressure
  * Still not strong enough to offset food deflation

---

#### (c) **Pan, Tobacco & Intoxicants**

* Inflation edged up slightly:

  * **3% (Nov)** vs **2.9% (Oct)**

---

#### (d) **Clothing & Footwear**

* Inflation eased marginally:

  * **1.5% (Nov)** vs **1.7% (Oct)**
* Reflects:

  * Stable demand
  * No major cost-push pressure

---

#### (e) **Housing**

* Inflation almost unchanged:

  * **2.95% (Nov)** vs **2.96% (Oct)**
* Indicates:

  * Stable rental and housing costs
  * No overheating in urban housing inflation

---

### 4. **Why Inflation Remains Very Low (Key Reasons)**

1. **High Base Effect**

   * Food inflation was very high in late 2024.
   * This suppresses current year-on-year inflation.

2. **Vegetable & Pulse Price Decline**

   * Seasonal supply improvement
   * Better market arrivals

3. **Limited Fuel Pass-through**

   * Fuel inflation rising, but still moderate

4. **Muted Demand-side Pressures**

   * Consumption recovery not overheating prices

---

### 5. **Mains Relevance (Analytical Perspective)**

**Positive Aspects**

* Low inflation supports:

  * Household purchasing power
  * Growth-friendly monetary policy
* Gives RBI space to:

  * Pause or cautiously adjust interest rates

**Concerns**

* Inflation is **statistically low**, not structurally low.
* Risks ahead:

  * Base effect will fade in 2026
  * Food prices are volatile
  * Rising fuel or global commodity prices could push inflation up

**Policy Implication**

* RBI must:

  * Focus more on **core inflation**
  * Avoid overreacting to temporary food deflation
* Government must:

  * Improve food supply chains
  * Stabilise edible oil prices

---

### 6. **Way Forward**

**Short-term**

* Maintain vigilance on:

  * Fuel inflation
  * Edible oil prices
* Avoid premature monetary loosening

**Medium-term**

* Reduce CPI distortion by:

  * Updating CPI base year (from 2012 to 2024 ‚Äì expected soon)
  * Rebalancing food weight

**Long-term**

* Structural reforms in:

  * Agricultural storage
  * Market linkages
  * Crop diversification

# Civil society group flags concerns over Karnataka hate speech Bill
## Karnataka Hate Speech and Hate Crimes (Prevention) Bill, 2025

---

### 1. **Basic Understanding**

**Hate Speech & Hate Crime**

* **Hate speech**: Expression that incites hatred, discrimination or violence against individuals/groups based on identity (religion, caste, gender, sexuality, etc.).
* **Hate crime**: A criminal act motivated by prejudice against a protected characteristic.
  üëâ Internationally, hate crime usually includes **both speech and physical acts**.

**Constitutional Context**

* **Article 19(1)(a)**: Freedom of speech and expression
* **Article 19(2)**: Reasonable restrictions (public order, morality, etc.)
* **Article 14 & 15**: Equality and non-discrimination
* **Preamble**: Fraternity and dignity of the individual

**Need for Special Law**

* Existing provisions (IPC/BNS, IT Act) deal with hate indirectly.
* Lack of:

  * Clear definition
  * Victim-centric approach
  * Preventive and remedial framework

---

### 2. **The News (What has happened?)**

* **Karnataka Hate Speech and Hate Crimes (Prevention) Bill, 2025**:

  * Tabled in the **State Assembly**
  * Proposed to be introduced in the **Legislative Council**
* A civil society group, **Campaign Against Hate Speech**, has urged:

  * **Wider public consultation** before final passage
* While welcoming the **intent**, the organisation flagged **serious legal and constitutional concerns**.
* The group cited:

  * **Supreme Court‚Äôs emphasis on meaningful consultation**
  * **Pre-Legislative Consultation Policy (2014)**

---

### 3. **Key Provisions of the Bill (As Criticised)**

#### (a) **Definition of Hate Crime**

* Limited mainly to **communication of hate speech**
* Does **not include**:

  * Mob lynching
  * Physical violence
  * Social/economic boycotts
  * Punitive demolitions

üëâ Problem:

* Creates a **false equivalence** between speech and violent acts.
* Ignores lived realities of hate-based violence.

---

#### (b) **Punishment Framework**

* No **standalone punishment** clearly defined for hate speech.
* Relies on:

  * **Emotion-based definitions** (hurt feelings, outrage)
* Such definitions have historically led to:

  * Weak enforcement
  * Subjective interpretation

---

#### (c) **Nature of Offence**

* Hate crime classified as:

  * **Cognisable**
  * **Non-bailable**
* However:

  * **Range of punishment unclear**
  * Raises concerns of disproportionality and arbitrariness

---

#### (d) **Preventive Powers**

* Broad powers given to:

  * Executive Magistrate
  * Police
* Issues:

  * No clear definition of:

    * Scope of powers
    * Due process safeguards
* Risk:

  * Arbitrary preventive action
  * Chilling effect on free speech

---

#### (e) **Online Content Blocking**

* Designated officer can:

  * Remove or block online content
  * **Before trial**
* Concern:

  * Prior restraint
  * Violation of free speech principles
  * No judicial oversight specified

---

### 4. **Mains Relevance (Critical Analysis)**

#### A. **Speech vs Violence**

* Treating hate speech and physical violence identically:

  * Dilutes seriousness of violent hate crimes
  * Weakens victim justice
* International best practices:

  * Distinguish between:

    * Speech offences
    * Hate-motivated crimes

---

#### B. **Misuse Potential**

* Vague definitions + strong executive powers =

  * Scope for political or selective misuse
* Past experience:

  * Overbroad laws often target dissent rather than hate

---

#### C. **Constitutional Concerns**

* **Article 19(2)** allows restriction only on specific grounds.
* Prior restraint without safeguards:

  * Constitutionally suspect
* Supreme Court jurisprudence:

  * Laws affecting speech must be:

    * Clear
    * Narrowly tailored
    * Proportionate

---

#### D. **Democratic Process & Consultation**

* **Pre-Legislative Consultation Policy, 2014**:

  * Mandates public consultation for impactful laws
* Supreme Court:

  * Emphasised participatory law-making
* Bypassing consultation:

  * Weakens legitimacy
  * Increases litigation risk

---

### 5. **Way Forward**

**Short-term**

* Pause passage in Legislative Council
* Release Bill for:

  * Public comments
  * Stakeholder consultation (legal experts, civil society, minorities)

**Medium-term**

* Redefine hate crime to include:

  * Physical violence
  * Economic and social exclusion
* Clearly differentiate:

  * Hate speech
  * Hate-motivated offences

**Long-term**

* Ensure:

  * Judicial oversight for content blocking
  * Clear safeguards on preventive powers
* Align State law with:

  * Supreme Court free speech jurisprudence
  * International human rights standards

---

# ‚ÄãFTAs for a start
## India‚Äôs Free Trade Agreements (FTAs): Lessons, Limits and the Way Forward

---

### 1. **Basic Understanding**

**What are FTAs / RTAs?**

* **Free Trade Agreements (FTAs)** or **Regional Trade Agreements (RTAs)** reduce or eliminate tariffs and other trade barriers between partner countries.
* They also cover:

  * Services
  * Investment
  * Standards
  * Rules of origin
  * Non-tariff barriers (NTBs)

**India‚Äôs Current Position**

* As per **WTO**, India has entered into **20 FTAs/RTAs**.
* Excludes:

  * **India‚ÄìUK FTA (July 2025)**
  * **India‚ÄìEFTA Trade and Economic Partnership Agreement (Oct 2025)**
* Ongoing negotiations with:

  * **United States**
  * **European Union**
  * **Canada**
  * **Southern African Customs Union (SACU)**

**Strategic Context**

* Rising **U.S. tariffs (up to 50%)** on Indian exports
* Global trade fragmentation
* Need for **export diversification** and **GVC integration**

---

### 2. **The News (What has happened?)**

* India is **fast-tracking FTA negotiations** due to:

  * Trade pressures from advanced economies
  * Tariff and non-tariff barriers
* Reports suggested possible **re-engagement with RCEP**:

  * India exited in **2019** due to:

    * Farm sector concerns
    * Weak rules of origin
    * Fear of import surge (especially from China)
* Government clarification:

  * **No formal move to rejoin RCEP**
  * Only **consultative engagement**

---

### 3. **Experience with Earlier FTAs: What Went Wrong?**

#### (a) **Widening Trade Deficits**

**ASEAN**

* Trade deficit:

  * ~$10 billion (2017)
  * ~$44 billion (2023)

**Japan**

* Exports increased
* Imports of:

  * Capital-intensive
  * High-technology goods
* Result: Persistent deficit

**South Korea**

* Similar asymmetry in trade outcomes

---

#### (b) **Structural & Policy Reasons**

1. **Weak Negotiation of Non-Tariff Issues**

   * Inadequate:

     * Mutual Recognition Agreements (MRAs)
     * Standards & certification alignment
     * Rules of origin safeguards
   * Result:

     * Indian exports face compliance barriers
     * Imports flow easily into India

2. **Poor Sectoral Customisation**

   * FTAs not aligned with:

     * India‚Äôs comparative advantages
     * MSME capabilities
     * Labour-intensive sectors

3. **Limited Industry Consultation**

   * Negotiations lacked:

     * Deep engagement with exporters
     * Feedback from sectoral bodies

4. **Low Domestic Awareness**

   * Government failed to:

     * Popularise FTAs among exporters
     * Build capacity to utilise preferential tariffs
   * Partner countries used FTAs more effectively

---

### 4. **Course Correction: Signs of Improvement**

**Review of ASEAN, Japan, Korea FTAs**

* Led to:

  * Reassessment of rules of origin
  * Better safeguards

**India‚ÄìUAE CEPA**

* Example of improved design
* Outcomes:

  * **Non-oil trade ~ $100 billion in FY25** (DGFT data)
* Features:

  * Better sectoral balance
  * Stronger services access
  * Investment facilitation

---

### 5. **Mains Relevance (Analytical Perspective)**

#### A. **FTAs Are Not a Silver Bullet**

* FTAs only **open doors**
* Gains depend on:

  * Domestic competitiveness
  * Export readiness
  * Standards compliance

#### B. **Trade Diversification Needs Structural Change**

* Cannot rely only on:

  * Tariff concessions
* Requires:

  * Integration into **Global Value Chains (GVCs)**
  * Technology upgrading
  * Logistics efficiency

#### C. **Negotiating Strategy Must Be Partner-Specific**

**With the U.S.**

* Focus sectors:

  * Services
  * Seafood
  * Engineering goods
  * Textiles
* Must address:

  * Tariff peaks
  * SPS & TBT barriers

**With the EU**

* Key challenge:

  * **Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM)**
* Sensitive sectors:

  * Iron & steel
  * Cement
  * Aluminium
* Need:

  * Carbon accounting
  * Green transition support

---

### 6. **Way Forward**

#### **Short-term**

* Ensure FTAs are:

  * Carefully sequenced
  * Backed by industry consultation
* Strengthen negotiation on:

  * MRAs
  * Standards
  * Rules of origin

#### **Medium-term**

* Support exporters through:

  * Quality infrastructure (labs, testing)
  * Trade facilitation
  * Market intelligence
* Build awareness:

  * FTA utilisation handbooks
  * Sector-specific outreach

#### **Long-term**

* Deep structural reforms:

  * Manufacturing competitiveness
  * Skill development
  * Logistics & ports
* Align FTAs with:

  * Production Linked Incentive (PLI) schemes
  * Climate commitments
  * Digital trade readiness

---

### 7. **Conclusion**

* FTAs are **starting points, not endpoints**.
* Without:

  * Domestic capacity building
  * Export ecosystem support
  * Strategic negotiation
* FTAs risk becoming **import enablers rather than export drivers**.
* India‚Äôs next generation FTAs must be **development-oriented**, **data-driven**, and **industry-aligned**.

---

  * Equity
  * South‚ÄìSouth solidarity
  * Opposition to monopolisation by powerful states

  * Home to **~1/3 of global population**
  * Among the **most climate-vulnerable regions**

# Census 2027 to cost ‚Çπ11,718 crore, no separate budget earmarked for NPR
## Census of India 2027: Digital Turn, Caste Enumeration & Governance Implications

---

### 1. **Basic Understanding**

**What is the Census?**

* The Census of India is conducted under the **Census Act, 1948**.
* It is the **largest administrative and statistical exercise** in the world.
* Purpose:

  * Population size and distribution
  * Socio-economic characteristics
  * Basis for:

    * Policy formulation
    * Fiscal devolution
    * Welfare targeting
    * Electoral delimitation (after constitutional changes)

**National Population Register (NPR)**

* A register of **usual residents** in India.
* First prepared in **2010**, updated in **2015**.
* Contains data of about **119 crore residents**.
* NPR has been politically sensitive as it is seen as the **first step towards NRC**, though legally distinct.

---

### 2. **The News (What has happened?)**

* **Union Cabinet approved Census of India 2027**.
* **Total cost**: ‚Çπ11,718.24 crore.
* Key differences from earlier plans:

  * **No separate budgetary allocation for NPR** (unlike 2019).
  * Government clarified:

    * *No decision taken to update NPR* during this Census.
* Census 2021 was postponed due to **COVID-19**.
* Home Minister highlighted:

  * Census 2027 as a ‚Äúnew compass for development‚Äù
  * Alignment with **‚ÄòSabka Saath, Sabka Vikas‚Äô** and **Amrit Kaal** vision.

---

### 3. **Key Features of Census 2027**

#### (a) **First Digital Census**

* Data collection through:

  * **Mobile applications**
  * Dedicated digital portal:

    * **Census Management and Monitoring System (CMMS)**
* Enables:

  * Real-time monitoring
  * Faster processing
  * Reduced manual errors

---

#### (b) **Census-as-a-Service (CaaS)**

* Census data to be provided in:

  * **Clean**
  * **Machine-readable**
  * **Actionable** formats
* Ministries can directly use datasets for:

  * Targeted schemes
  * Evidence-based governance
* Marks a shift from:

  * Static reports ‚Üí **dynamic data infrastructure**

---

#### (c) **Caste Enumeration**

* **First time since Independence** that caste data will be collected in a national Census.
* Significance:

  * Evidence-based social justice policies
  * More accurate assessment of:

    * Backwardness
    * Representation gaps
* Sensitive but transformative for:

  * Reservation policy debates
  * Welfare targeting

---

#### (d) **Human Resources**

* About **18,600 technical personnel** engaged for ~**550 days**.
* Enumerators:

  * Mostly **government teachers**
  * Appointed by **State governments**
  * Census work in addition to regular duties

---

### 4. **What is Missing This Time? (NPR Aspect)**

* **2019 Cabinet decision**:

  * ‚Çπ3,941.35 crore for NPR update
  * ‚Çπ8,754.23 crore for Census 2021
* **2025‚Äì26 decision**:

  * No NPR allocation
  * Explicit statement:

    * *No decision to update NPR with Census 2027*
* Implication:

  * Addresses public concerns linking Census to NRC
  * Politically and socially significant reassurance

---

### 5. **Mains Relevance (Analytical Perspective)**

#### A. **Governance & Federalism**

* Census data underpins:

  * Centre‚ÄìState resource allocation

#### B. **Digital Governance**

### 6. **Way Forward**

**Short-term**
